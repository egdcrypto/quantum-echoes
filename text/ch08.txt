Chapter 6

TWO YEARS IN SHADOW


I've lost count.

Not of the dimensions. The HUD tracks those. Dimension 213. Two hundred and thirteen realities visited, catalogued, survived. Two hundred and thirteen test breaths. Two hundred and thirteen rolls of the cosmic dice.

What I've lost count of is the time.

The HUD has a clock, but the clock doesn't connect to anything. There's no Monday in the multiverse. No January. No birthday. Just the jump, and the breath, and the cat, and the next jump.

I know it's been roughly two years. I know because I've filled four notebooks -- scavenged from various Earth-like dimensions -- with observations. The first notebook has clear handwriting and scientific notation. The fourth looks like it was written by a man who is very tired and possibly losing his mind.

Accurate.

Schrodinger is the only constant. He sleeps in the pod when I sleep. He eats what I eat, plus whatever small creatures he catches in the habitable dimensions.

He has not aged.

I noticed this around dimension 80. Pulled out the photo I took in the early days -- Schrodinger asleep on the pod's instrument panel, one ear folded, patch of white on his chest. Compared it to the cat sitting in front of me. Identical. Same muscle tone. Same fur density. Same weight distribution in his face.

Cats age. Even healthy cats. In two years you'd see something -- a slight dullness in the coat, a little less elasticity in the skin, maybe a change in the eye. Especially the one good eye.

Nothing.

I compared photos of myself next. Same result. I've been sleeping in a pod for two years, eating scavenged food, breathing uncertain air, running on adrenaline and stubbornness. I should look like a disaster. I don't. Same face. Same forty-three-year-old frame I had the night the State Bridge fired.

I spent three days trying to explain this. Ran through every hypothesis I could think of. Relativistic time dilation? No -- I'm not moving at significant fractions of light speed. Metabolic suspension during transit? Possible, but the math doesn't cover the periods I spend in-dimension. Dimensional time offset? Doesn't explain why I'm not aging while physically present in a given reality.

The transit keeps our biology frozen somehow. I've confirmed it across dozens of photo comparisons. Filed it under Things That Should Be Impossible But Apparently Aren't. That file is getting thick.

There's a connection I keep almost seeing. Schrodinger was on the State Bridge when it fired. He exists somewhere in both realities -- the one I left and wherever I am now. Like Schrodinger's actual thought experiment, except real. And I'm traveling with him. Using a machine built on the same principles that split him.

Is his superposition the reason we don't age?

I don't have the instruments to test it. I don't have the theory to frame it. I just have a cat who hasn't aged and a face in the mirror that hasn't changed and a feeling like I'm looking at a puzzle with half the pieces missing.

Filed it. Moved on.

He's gained weight, though. I have no idea how. Interdimensional travel should not be fattening, but the cat has found a way. Schrodinger Rating on his own metabolism: 11/10.

The exhaustion is in my head. Not my body -- my body is fine, preserved, the same frame I had the night everything went wrong. But my mind is worn down to the studs. Two hundred and thirteen dimensions, and not one of them is home.

I've stopped believing I'll find home.

The only thing keeping me jumping is the fear that if I stop, I'll sit in the pod and never move again.

Go-bag packed. Sharpie rules worn but legible. Atmospheric sensor on its third rebuild.

I am a well-equipped man with nowhere to go.


                                *


Dimension 214.

Hold breath. Activate. Lurch.

Light settles. Test breath.

Clean. Rich. Earth-standard. Sensor confirms: twenty-one percent oxygen, negligible CO2 elevation, no detectable toxins.

Schrodinger is already out, sniffing the ground. Ears up. Tail relaxed. Maximum confidence.

Residential neighborhood. Quiet street. Trees. Sidewalks. Houses with lawns and mailboxes. Subdivision, late 1990s. A sprinkler chattering on someone's lawn. Light is late afternoon, golden, slanting through trees at an angle that says October.

Almost-home.

I hate almost-homes.

You know the feeling when a word is on the tip of your tongue? Almost-homes are like that, except the word is "home" and the tip of your tongue is an entire reality that's ninety-five percent right and five percent wrong in ways that make your stomach hurt.

But the air is perfect, and Schrodinger is already three houses down investigating a garden gnome, so I secure the pod -- carport of a house that looks unoccupied -- and shoulder the go-bag.

Newspaper. Gas station. Any source that tells me where and when.

I walk to the end of the block. Small park. Playground, benches, walking trail through a stand of live oaks.

Texas live oaks.

I'd know them anywhere.

My chest does something involuntary. Not pain. Not hope. Something in between.

I've been in Texas-like dimensions before. This one feels different. Closer. The trees are right. The light is right. The smell -- mesquite and cedar and the dusty sweetness of Central Texas in autumn.

Community notice board at the park entrance. School fundraiser flyer. Lost dog poster. Schedule for the Bastrop County Library.

Bastrop.

I'm in Bastrop, Texas.

I lived thirty miles from Bastrop in my home dimension. Lawson grew up an hour from here.

Okay. Deep breath. Stay on task.


                                *


Gas station. Newspapers behind the counter, but there's a digital date display above the register.

November 3, 2023.

One year behind my home dimension.

Close. So close.

I buy a paper with cash from three dimensions ago. Bills are close enough to pass casual inspection. The cashier doesn't look twice. I sit on the curb outside and read.

Local news. State news. National. Everything is almost right. President is different -- a name I don't recognize. Sports scores are wrong in the specific way that tells me this history diverged sometime in the past, and small differences compounded into a world that's ninety-five percent mine and five percent uncanny.

Page seven.

Small article. Local interest. A memorial event at the Thorne Institute for Advanced Physics, honoring the late Dr. Henry Taylor, who passed away six months ago from a sudden cardiac event.

I read the sentence three times.

The late Dr. Henry Taylor.

Passed away.

Six months ago.

This dimension's Henry Taylor is dead.


                                *


I sit on the curb for a long time.

Schrodinger finds me twenty minutes later, having completed his perimeter survey of the gas station. He sits beside me and stares at the newspaper with his one eye.

"There's a me here. There was a me here. And he's dead."

Blink.

Sudden cardiac event. Heart attack. Forty-three years old, apparently healthy, collapsed in his lab at the Thorne Institute. No warning signs. No pre-existing conditions.

Just dead.

Forty-three. My age. The same age I've been for two years, frozen in some way I still can't explain, while this version of me aged normally and then died.

I think about Thorne. His name in over two hundred dimensions. His face on every front page. His Institute everywhere.

And now here, a Henry Taylor is dead at the Thorne Institute.

Sudden cardiac event.

The words sit wrong. They taste like the metallic air of the alien cave -- something my body recognizes as false before my mind catches up.

I'm a physicist, not a detective. I don't have evidence. Don't have a motive. Just a feeling -- the same instinct that told me to write the note, the same tug that told me to grab the cat.

A feeling that "sudden cardiac event" is a euphemism.

That this Henry Taylor didn't just die.

That he was killed.

And I know, with the certainty of a man who has seen Elias Thorne's name in two hundred and thirteen dimensions, who killed him.


                                *


I go to the memorial.

This is the stupidest thing I've done since grabbing a cat off a quantum state bridge and riding a superposition wave into an alien dimension.

I look exactly like the man they're memorializing. Same face, same build, same everything -- because I haven't aged, because whatever is keeping my biology on hold hasn't stopped, because I am a walking dead man's double.

But I have to see.

Hat. Sunglasses. Jacket with the collar turned up. Go-bag in the car I've "borrowed" -- multiverse traveler's word for "taken from an empty driveway in a dimension where I'm pretty sure no one's coming back for it."

The memorial is at the Thorne Institute. Which exists here too. Of course it does. Gleaming glass and steel. Almost identical to the one in my home dimension. Same architecture. Same signage. Same gravitational pull warping the town around it.

Maybe a hundred people. Colleagues, friends, community members. Small stage in the atrium. Podium with flowers. Photograph of this dimension's Henry Taylor.

I stop breathing.

It's me.

Same face. Same crooked smile. Same eyes. His hair is parted on the left -- I part mine on the right. Otherwise it's a mirror.

I stand at the back, behind a pillar, hat low.

People speak. Colleagues talk about his brilliance, his absent-mindedness. The coffee mugs. The chalkboard equations. The way he'd forget to eat. Same jokes my colleagues would tell about me.

Same man.

Then someone walks to the podium who makes everything in my head stop.


                                *


She's older than I remember.

That's the first thought, and it's a dumb thought, because of course she's older. The Gayelyn I knew was thirty-one when the cancer took her. That was sixteen years ago in my personal timeline. This Gayelyn is mid-forties.

She looks like Gayelyn.

Not the memory version. Not the one filtered through grief and nostalgia until the real person disappears. The real version. Present tense. Alive. Standing at a podium with tears on her face and a piece of paper she's clearly been rehearsing all morning and is still going to struggle through.

Hair shorter than I remember. Lines around her eyes I never got to watch form. Dark blue dress. A gold pendant necklace I don't recognize -- something this dimension's Henry gave her, a piece of their shared life with no analog in mine.

She speaks.

Her voice.

Same. Maybe deeper, or maybe I'm hearing it with different ears -- ears that have been starving for this specific sound for sixteen years.

She talks about Henry. Her Henry. And every word could be about me. His humor. His obsessive work habits. The way he left little notes in books for her to find.

My knees stop working. I grab the pillar.

"He left notes." Voice catching. "Little scraps of paper in the books on our shelf. I keep finding them. 'Don't forget you're extraordinary.' 'The universe is weird and so are we.' It's like he knew... like he wanted me to keep finding pieces of him, even after..."

She can't finish.

I wrote those notes too. Same words. Same habit. I left them for Lawson. He left them for Gayelyn. Two Henry Taylors in two realities, expressing love the exact same way. Scraps of paper in books.

Then, from the second row, a young man stands up and walks to the podium. Puts his arm around Gayelyn. She leans into him.

Tall. Brown hair. Familiar jaw. Familiar eyes. He looks like Lawson.

Not like Lawson. Like Lawson. Same bones, same proportions, same expressions translated into a different key. My daughter, refracted through different chromosomes.

Maybe nineteen. Same age Lawson was when I vanished.

Their son.

This dimension's Lawson.


                                *


I leave before it ends.

I make it to the car. Sit in the driver's seat. Hands on the wheel.

I shake.

Not crying. Past crying. The kind of shaking that happens when your body is processing something that doesn't fit anywhere, something that rewrites your internal wiring.

Gayelyn is alive.

In two hundred and thirteen dimensions, across two years of jumping, across a distance that can't be measured in any unit humans have, my wife is alive.

She's older. She's grieving. She's wearing a necklace I didn't give her.

And she's alive.

And she's alone. Because the Henry Taylor of this dimension is dead. Killed, I believe, by Thorne. Left with a son who looks like my daughter and a life that's been gutted.

The thought arrives fully formed, like it's been building itself in the background for an hour:

I could stay.

I could stay here. Where Gayelyn is alive and Henry Taylor is dead and there is a space in the world shaped exactly like me.

The thought is monstrous. It's also the most human thought I've had in two years.

Schrodinger climbs into my lap from the back seat. Settles on my thighs, warmth spreading through the denim.

He purrs.

First time he's purred spontaneously. Not because I'm petting him, not because he's being fed. Just... content. As if something has shifted.

Schrodinger Rating on this dimension: unknown. The cat seems to think we're home.


                                *


I don't approach her that day.

Or the next.

Or the next.

I find a cabin outside town. Empty. Deep in the woods. Nobody seems to own it. I secure the pod in a lean-to out back, covered with a tarp. Stock the cabin from the go-bag, supplement with careful trips to a grocery store two towns over, where nobody knows the face of the dead physicist from the Thorne Institute.

And I watch.

I learn Gayelyn's routine.

She walks in the mornings. A trail through the wooded park on the outskirts of town, same park I saw the day I arrived. She goes alone. Sometimes she brings a dog -- a golden retriever I don't recognize, a member of her life that has no counterpart in mine. She visits a small bookstore on Tuesday afternoons. Picks up Lawson from community college on Wednesdays and Fridays.

Lawson. The boy who shares my daughter's name and face.

I watch him too. From a distance. He moves differently -- longer stride, more angular, the physicality of a young man instead of a young woman. But the mannerisms are identical. The head tilt when he's thinking. The way he pushes his hair back with his left hand. The way he carries a book pressed against his chest instead of dangling at his side.

My Lawson does all of those things.

Same DNA, expressed differently. Nature and nurture tangled together, producing two people who are simultaneously the same and completely different.

I am stalking my own family.

That's the word for it. I know it. The fact that they're not technically my family doesn't change the behavior. I am a man hiding in the woods, watching a woman and her son through binoculars I scavenged from a dimension where the air tasted like copper.

If a cop stopped me right now, I would not have a good explanation.

"I'm her dead husband from a parallel universe" is not a viable defense in any jurisdiction.


                                *


While I watch, I investigate.

The physicist in me can't leave it alone. The "sudden cardiac event" doesn't sit right.

Library terminals. Online news archives. The Thorne Institute's website has a memorial page for Henry Taylor -- generous in praise, thin on specifics. The obituary, written by a colleague, mentions the suddenness, the shock, the absence of symptoms.

No autopsy mentioned. Not unusual for a cardiac event. But notable.

I approach it like a scientist, not a detective. Look at the data. Ask: does this fit the model?

Henry Taylor, forty-three. No history of heart disease. Published a paper six days before his death -- no cognitive decline. Gave a lecture two days before. Attended a faculty meeting the morning of. By all accounts, healthy and working normally.

Then, between 4:15 and 4:45 p.m. on a Wednesday in May, dead.

Security logs -- public, part of the incident report -- show he badged into his lab at 2:30 p.m. Next badge event: security team entering at 5:10 p.m. after he failed to sign out.

Two hours and forty minutes. Alone in a lab. At the Thorne Institute.

I think about what can happen to a man alone in a lab at the Thorne Institute.

I think about Thorne in two hundred and thirteen dimensions.

I think: he did this.

Can't prove it. No forensics, no witnesses, no confession. Just a pattern and an instinct and two years of watching the same man show up in every reality like a virus in the operating system.

And a question I'm afraid to ask: is that why I'm here? Did the DNA trail lead me to this dimension not because my father passed through, but because a version of me died here?

Am I tracing life or death?


                                *


Three weeks.

I've been here three weeks. Hiding in a cabin. Watching a woman I loved. Love. Have never stopped loving. The decision is forming the way crystals form in a supersaturated solution. Slowly. One molecule at a time.

I could leave. I should leave. The rational thing is to get back in the pod, roll the dice, keep jumping. Don't interfere. Don't insert yourself into a life that isn't yours. Don't pretend that sharing his DNA gives you any right to her grief or her love or her son.

I know all of this.

I know it the way I know standing in a superposition field is dangerous. Knowing doesn't change what you do when the light reaches for you.

I'm tired.

Two years tired. Two hundred and thirteen dimensions tired. Tired of holding my breath. Tired of the test breath. Tired of the dice. Tired of waking up in the pod with the cat on my chest and not knowing where I am or when I am or whether the air will kill me.

And Gayelyn is alive. She's alive and walking alone in a park every morning.

And I am thirty minutes away.

And Lawson is here. A boy who lost his father to a "heart attack" I'm certain was murder. Navigating the same grief my Lawson navigated when I vanished.

There is a space in this world shaped like me.

And I am so tired of not filling it.


                                *


But.

Lawson.

My Lawson. She's in the original dimension. Nineteen, or twenty, or twenty-one -- time moves differently and I don't know the conversion rate. She's holding a note that says it's Schrodinger and trying to make sense of it. Every day I stay here is a day I'm not looking for a way home.

Except I haven't been looking for a way home. Not for a long time. I've been looking for a reason to keep jumping. And now I've found something worse than an answer: a temptation.

Stay. Stop jumping. Stop running. Fill the space.

If I stay, I'm choosing Gayelyn over Lawson. A wife I lost over a daughter I left.

That's the math.

I've never been good at the equations that matter. The human ones. The ones that can't be solved with variables and constants, only with choices.

Schrodinger sits on the cabin's porch railing, watching the sunset. He's found a sunny spot. The squirrels are entertaining. The air is perfect. The nearest threat is the raccoon that raids the trash can on Thursdays.

He's more content than I've seen him in months.

I tell myself: I'll approach Gayelyn. Just talk to her. See if it's even possible. See if she can look at my face -- her dead husband's face -- without screaming or calling the police or breaking in a way that can't be fixed.

My rational mind says leave. Get in the pod. Don't do this to her. Don't do this to yourself. Every logical argument points away from that trail.

But there's the other thing. The compulsion. Quiet, insistent, below the level of logic. The same kind that moved my hand across a piece of paper in a kitchen sixteen years ago, writing nine words I didn't plan. It says stay. It says go to the trail. I don't understand it. I don't trust it, exactly. But I've learned, across two hundred and thirteen dimensions, that the compulsion has never been wrong.

If she can't, I'll leave. Pod. Dice. Gone.

If she can...

I don't finish that thought.


                                *


The walking trail. Morning.

October light, slanting and golden. I've been to other worlds. Most of them aren't better.

I've watched her walk this trail nine times. She always pauses at a bend where two live oaks arch overhead. Touches the bark of the larger one. A ritual.

I'm waiting at the bend, heart rate doing things that belong in a medical textbook, when Schrodinger -- who I left at the cabin, explicitly instructed to stay at the cabin, clearly and firmly told to remain at the cabin -- trots out of the underbrush and sits at my feet.

"You were supposed to stay home."

Blink.

The blink says: And you were supposed to be dead. We're both somewhere we shouldn't be.

Fair point.

Footsteps. Soft crunch of leaves. Measured pace.

She comes around the bend.

She sees the cat first.

This is important. If she'd seen me first -- a man who looks exactly like her dead husband, standing on her trail -- she would have run. Or screamed. Or frozen. Any sane person would.

But she sees the cat. A one-eyed gray tabby sitting on the trail like he owns it.

And she laughs.

Small. Surprised. A little broken. The laugh of a woman who has been walking alone for six months and just been confronted by a cat who is clearly judging her pace.

"Well, hello." Crouches down. Extends her hand. The cat walks forward and rubs against her fingers. "Where did you come from, handsome?"

"He's mine."

The words are out before I can stop them. Too direct. Too sudden. She looks up.

Her face.

I've imagined this moment for sixteen years. In the worst hours, in the bottom of the loneliness, in dimensions where the air was thin and the only things keeping me alive were the cat and the go-bag and the Sharpie rules on the pod wall.

I was not prepared.

She goes white. Blood drains from her cheeks. Her eyes -- brown, flecked with gold, the same eyes I fell in love with in a university library -- go wide.

Her mouth opens. Nothing comes out.

"I'm sorry." Hands up, stepping back. "I'm sorry. I know what this looks like--"

She screams.

Not a word. Not my name. Raw. Animal. From somewhere deeper than language. She stumbles backward, catches herself on the oak she always touches.

"Stay away from me." Shaking so badly I can barely understand her. "Stay away. I don't know what this is. I don't know who you are. Stay away."

"Gayelyn--"

"Don't." Fumbling in her pocket. Phone. "Don't say my name. I'm calling the police."

"Please. Please listen."

"You look like my husband." Crying now, and the tears are fury, not grief. "My husband is dead. He is dead. And you look exactly like him, and I don't know what kind of sick-- who sent you?"

"No one sent me."

"Then what ARE you?"

The question hangs. Schrodinger sits between us, looking from her to me and back. Tail twitches once.

"I'm Henry. I'm not your Henry. But I am Henry. And I know that doesn't make any sense."

She stares at me. Phone in her hand. Thumb on the screen. I can see the numbers: 9-1-1, half-dialed.

"Get away from me." Flat. Controlled. Holding herself together by force of will. "I don't care who you are. Get away from me, or I call this in, and you can explain to the Bastrop police why you look exactly like a dead man."

I step back. Another step. Another.

"Okay. I'm going. I'm sorry."

She watches me retreat. Hand on the phone. Eyes on my face. And I can see it: a woman who was just starting, after six months, to learn how to breathe around the hole in her life.

And I just ripped it open.

I take Schrodinger and I leave.


                                *


Back at the cabin. Sitting on the porch.

I shake for about an hour.

Schrodinger sits on the railing with the expression of a creature who has no idea what just happened but is fairly certain it could have gone better. Schrodinger Rating on my social skills: 2/10.

I should leave. Get in the pod. Jump. Never look at this dimension again.

I just terrorized a grieving woman. Made her think she was losing her mind, or that someone was playing a cruel joke, or that the universe was punishing her for something she didn't do.

I should leave.

I don't leave.


                                *


Three days later, I go back to the trail.

Not to approach her. Just to see if she's there. Just to see if I broke something that can't be repaired.

She's there. Same time. Same route. But she's not alone. The golden retriever is with her. She's walking faster. Eyes scanning the trees.

I stay hidden. She finishes her walk. Gets in her car. Drives away.

I stand in the trees feeling like the worst person in any dimension.

Next morning: she's there again. Still the dog. Still the watchful eyes. But she pauses at the oak. Touches the bark. Does her ritual.

Morning after that, I find something on the bench nearest the two oaks. A piece of paper, folded once, held down by a rock.

I approach it like it might explode.

Neat, careful handwriting: "If you are real, and not something my grief invented, prove it. Tell me something only Henry would know. Leave it here. If you are not real, if this is a trick or a scam or something worse, know that I have a gun in my bedside table and my son was a linebacker and I will not be kind about it."

I read it three times.

She didn't call the police. She came back.

And she left a test.

I sit on the bench. Schrodinger jumps up beside me. I pull a pen from the go-bag -- a Pilot G-2, the kind I've been using for twenty years across at least three realities -- and turn the paper over.

What would Henry know? What would any Henry know?

I write: "You have a birthmark on your left shoulder blade shaped like a comma. You are self-conscious about it. You shouldn't be. The first time I saw it, I told you it looked like the universe's punctuation mark, and you told me that was the worst line you'd ever heard, and then you kissed me anyway. I built you a hummingbird feeder out of a Florence flask and copper wire for our second anniversary. You said it was the most romantic thing anyone had ever made from lab equipment."

I fold the paper. Put it under the rock. Leave.


                                *


Next morning, she's sitting on the bench when I arrive.

No dog. No phone. Just Gayelyn, wearing a coat against the October chill, staring at the trail.

I stop at the edge of the clearing. Schrodinger walks directly to her and sits at her feet.

She looks down at him. Then up at me.

Her face is not what I expected. Not terror. Not acceptance. The expression of a person presented with evidence of something impossible who has decided to hear the argument before rendering judgment.

"The hummingbird feeder." Her voice is steady. Barely. "No one knew about that. I never told anyone. It broke three years ago and I cried for two days and told Lawson I had allergies."

I don't move.

"Sit down." Not an invitation. A command.

I sit. Far end of the bench.

"Start talking. And I swear to God, if you can't make me understand what I'm looking at, I will make good on every threat I wrote on that piece of paper."

I believe her.

I start talking.


                                *


I tell her everything.

Not the smooth version. Not the one with dramatic pauses. The messy version. The one where I stumble over the physics because I've been talking to a cat for two years and my ability to explain quantum mechanics to another human has atrophied considerably.

"So there's this field. Zero-point energy. It's everywhere, it's in everything, it's the -- okay, think of it like the background hum of the universe. And my colleague, Elias Thorne, figured out how to use it to bridge between realities. Like tuning a radio to a different station except the station is an entirely different version of everything."

"You're telling me you're from a parallel universe."

"I'm telling you I'm from a parallel universe."

"Henry." She says it before she can stop herself. Then catches it. "The Henry I knew -- my Henry -- he talked about this. Theoretical stuff. After-dinner, two-glasses-of-wine stuff. When he'd get that look."

"The one where he looks like he's seeing through the wall."

"Yes." Her voice cracks. "That one."

Silence. Schrodinger has migrated to the bench between us. Body pointed toward her. Eye pointed toward me. Switzerland.

"I don't believe you."

"I know."

"I'm not sure I don't believe you, either."

"I know that too."

"The hummingbird feeder. The birthmark." Pause. "You could have found those out. Somehow."

"You're right."

She looks at me then. Really looks. "So tell me something you couldn't have found out. Something nobody could know."

I think.

"When we were twenty-three. Before we got married. You had a panic attack in the parking lot of the Olive Garden on Route 35. You'd never had one before. You thought you were dying. I held you in the back seat of my car for forty-five minutes and you made me promise not to tell anyone."

She is very still.

"And the thing you said after, when it was over, wasn't 'thank you' or 'I'm sorry.' You said, 'Well, that was stupid.' And then you asked if we could still get breadsticks."

Nothing.

"You never told anyone. Neither did I."

Something changes in her face. Not softening. A crack in a wall, and light getting in.

"You should go. I need to think."

I stand up.

"Come back tomorrow."

I look at her.

"Come back tomorrow. Same time. And bring the cat."


                                *


That was the beginning.

Not of understanding. That would take weeks of talking on that bench, in the cold October mornings that became cold November mornings, with Schrodinger between us like a furry demilitarized zone.

There were days she was angry. Days she said she was done. Days she didn't show up and I sat on the bench alone wondering if I should get in the pod.

There was one afternoon, second week, she showed up with red eyes and told me she'd spent the night going through photo albums, comparing his face to the image of me in her head.

"You don't get to just show up." Her voice is raw. "You don't get to appear on a trail and be him. He was mine. His life was mine. His death was mine. And you come here from -- what, another dimension -- and you wear his face and you know about the hummingbird feeder and you just, what? Expect me to be okay?"

"No. I don't expect you to be okay with it."

"Good. Because I'm not."

"I know."

"I am very, very not okay with it, Henry."

But she kept coming back.

One shared memory at a time. One detail too personal to be coincidence. One morning where she laughed at something I said and then looked horrified that she'd laughed.

Not trust. Not yet. Something more cautious. The way you extend a hand toward an animal you're not sure about.


                                *


I knew I was going to stay.

Not because it was right. My daughter was in another dimension, holding a note, searching for a trail I'd left.

Not because it was fair. It wasn't fair to anyone. Not to Gayelyn, who deserved to grieve without a ghost on her walking trail. Not to Lawson, the son, who deserved a father and not a duplicate. Not to my Lawson, who deserved a father who came home.

I stayed because I was tired and broken and the woman I loved was alive.

I am not strong enough to lose her twice.

That's not the noble truth. Not the scientific truth. The human one.

I am not strong enough to lose her twice.

And so I stayed.

And somewhere, in a dimension I couldn't reach, my daughter was standing in an empty lab, holding a note, beginning the search that would carry her across the multiverse to find a father who stopped looking for home because he'd found something he couldn't leave.

I hope she'll understand.

I hope she'll forgive me.

I don't think she will.

Schrodinger is purring. Gayelyn is saying my name. The raccoon got into the trash again last night and I need to fix the latch.

For the first time in two years, I'm not holding my breath.
