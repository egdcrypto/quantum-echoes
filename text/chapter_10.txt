CHAPTER 10 - THE MURDER


I go back.

Every rule I wrote for myself says don't. Rule four, actually. Written in Sharpie on the inside of the pod, back when I still used the pod: Do not return to a dimension where you've been embedded.

Good rule. Smart rule.

I go back anyway.

Because I left something in dimension twenty-two. Not a piece of equipment. A person. A version of my father, mid-thirties, pre-Thorne, alive and walking around using M&Ms to explain wave-particle duality to confused biochemists. I watched Thorne size him up in a hallway like he was deciding whether to keep a tool or throw it away, and I left without saying anything.

Seven weeks ago, my timeline.

Seven weeks of other dimensions, other Thornes. The park. The child. "Mama." All of it piling up in my head like unsorted data.

And underneath all that, a thing I can't shake. It shows up when I'm brushing my teeth. Waiting for coffee. That half-second between sleep and awake when your brain hasn't started lying to you yet.

Thorne's face in the hallway. The assessment. The math he was doing behind his eyes.

I need to know if this dimension's Henry Taylor is still alive.


*


I think: dimension twenty-two. Pathway locked.

The Bridge wraps me. Antimatter field, the hum against my skin. Kitchen dissolves.

I land in the alley behind the coffee shop where I built my cover. Two blocks from the Thorne Institute. Dumpster. Rain. Normal.

Something is wrong.

The Bridge does this thing when there's danger. A cold pulse behind my sternum, sharp, like someone pressing a fist against the inside of my ribcage. I've felt it in dimensions with toxic air, unstable energy signatures. It means: pay attention, idiot.

The subconscious thread is running. Processing the local quantum fabric, ambient energy, DNA traces. And what it's finding doesn't match what I left seven weeks ago.

Henry Taylor's DNA resonance has shifted.

Not shifted as in moved locations. Shifted as in degrading. Fading. The way a biological signature fades when the source stops generating it.

When the source is dead.

No.

I walk fast toward the Institute.


*


The lobby tells me everything I need to know and nothing I want to know.

Memorial display. His photograph, the one from the faculty page. Lopsided grin. Eyes that look like mine. Flowers. A small sign:

In memory of Dr. Henry Taylor, 1981-2024. Beloved colleague and friend.

I stop walking. People flow around me. Someone says "excuse me." I don't move.

Seven weeks. He was alive seven weeks ago. I watched him explain physics with candy and I thought: this is the dad I lost. And I left. And now he's dead.

The sign doesn't say how. They never do. "Beloved colleague." "Deeply missed." Flowers around a photograph. No mention of whether the man died in his sleep or was killed by the person whose name is on the building.

Okay. I'm a ghost in this dimension -- no Lawson Taylor exists here -- but I'm also a former employee. Cover identity still in the system. Badge in my pocket.

And it turns out grief is an excellent disguise. A woman with red eyes walking the halls of a building that just lost a colleague doesn't attract suspicion. She attracts sympathy.

I badge in.


*


Break room bulletin board. The internal newsletter.

Dr. Henry Taylor, senior researcher, passed away unexpectedly on October 14th. Cause of death: sudden cardiac arrest. He was 43 years old.

I read it twice.

Sudden cardiac arrest. Forty-three years old.

Same words. Same age. Same cause as the Henry Taylor in my father's adopted dimension. The one Gayelyn mourned. The one whose memorial I hid in the back of.

Two Henry Taylors. Two dimensions. Both working at Thorne Institutes. Both dead of sudden cardiac arrest at forty-three. Both healthy, no prior heart conditions.

I'm a physicist. I know coincidences exist.

But they don't look like this.


*


His lab is locked. Standard procedure when a lab's not in use. My badge has Level 2 access, which covers the research wing.

Swipe. Green light. Door opens.

The lab looks almost exactly like my father's lab in our home dimension. Same layout, same equipment. Whiteboard covered in equations. Three coffee mugs at various stages of archaeological decay. Papers everywhere.

I don't touch anything. I photograph everything with the disposable phone I bought in this dimension. Whiteboard, desk, equipment, papers. I'm not a forensic scientist. But I'm a physicist, and I know the difference between a lab where someone was working and a lab where something went wrong.

This is a lab where someone was working.

No scorch marks. No damaged equipment. No signs of power surge, electrical fault, none of the things that might kill a healthy forty-three-year-old. Everything intact, everything functioning. Except the guy who worked here is dead.

On the desk: a notebook, open. I recognize the equations. Extensions of the macroscopic superposition framework.

Wait.

I look closer.

This Henry was close. Really close. Maybe weeks from a working theory for the Quantum State Bridge.

I step back from the desk.

Close enough to be dangerous. Close enough that if someone wanted to be the only person who understood this technology -- someone who saw a collaborator getting too independent, too near a breakthrough that didn't need the patron anymore --

That someone might decide the collaborator was no longer an asset.


*


I find the footage in the security office.

This is the dumbest thing I've done in any dimension. The security office is a small room at the end of the admin wing, staffed during business hours by a guard who is currently not here. "BACK IN 15" taped to the door.

Door's unlocked. Because security at the Institute is designed to keep outsiders out, not to stop insiders from accessing the surveillance system. And I'm an insider. Badge, ID, puffy eyes.

I sit at the terminal. Pull up October 14th. Henry Taylor's lab.

Here's what the footage shows. I'm going to be clinical about this because if I'm not clinical about it I'm going to break.

Timestamp: 2:17 p.m. Henry is at his desk. Working. Coffee mug in hand. Papers spread out. He's writing in the notebook -- the same one I just photographed. Body language: relaxed, focused. A man doing the work he loves.

3:44 p.m. A figure enters the lab.

I know who it is before the camera catches his face. The posture. Upright, precise. The walk -- measured, deliberate, each step placed with an intentionality that normal people reserve for walking on ice.

Elias Thorne.

Henry looks up. Smiles.

He smiles at the man who is about to kill him. Easy, warm, the smile of a scientist greeting the guy who funds his work.

They talk. No audio. The Institute's security cameras are video-only. A privacy measure that suddenly feels less like policy and more like planning.

They talk for roughly eight minutes. Henry gestures at the whiteboard. Thorne nods. Henry shows him something in the notebook. Thorne leans in.

3:53 p.m. Thorne places his hand on Henry's shoulder.

The gesture looks friendly. Paternal, even.

Henry's body goes rigid.

It's subtle. If you weren't looking for it -- if you were a security guard reviewing footage, or a cop investigating a natural death -- you'd miss it. Shoulders stiffen. Hand on the coffee mug trembles. The mug doesn't fall. Not yet.

Thorne's hand stays on Henry's shoulder for four seconds.

Then he removes his hand. Steps back. Straightens his jacket. Henry is still sitting. Leaning slightly forward. One hand on the desk, the other on the mug. He looks like a man who got punched in the chest but hasn't processed it yet.

Thorne walks to the door. Turns back. Says something. Expression: neutral, composed. The face of a man leaving a routine meeting.

He leaves.

3:54 p.m. Forty-three seconds after Thorne walks out. Henry drops the coffee mug. It shatters. He reaches for the desk. Misses. Left arm goes to his chest.

3:55. He falls.

3:55 and fourteen seconds. He stops moving.

The lab is empty. Coffee spreads across the floor. The equations on the whiteboard are the last ones he'll ever write.

Nobody enters again until 5:10 p.m., when a colleague comes looking for Henry about a meeting.

Sudden cardiac arrest. Forty-three years old.


*


I watch the footage three times.

First time: I'm watching Henry. My father's face on another man. Alive and working at 2:17. Dead at 3:55. The first viewing is just grief. I can't see straight. My vision whites out at the edges and I have to grip the desk.

Second time: I'm watching Thorne. The hand on the shoulder. The four seconds. The neutral face as he walks out. I'm trying to figure out the mechanism. What was transmitted through that contact? What induces cardiac arrest through brief skin contact and leaves nothing an autopsy would find?

I don't know. I'm not a cardiologist.

But I'm a physicist who's studied zero-point energy for sixteen years. I know the ZPE field interacts with biological systems in ways conventional medicine doesn't detect.

So. Could Thorne disrupt cardiac electrical function through a focused ZPE pulse? Something delivered through touch? Something that mimics a natural cardiac event?

With what he knows? With his resources?

Yeah. It's possible. Worse than possible. It's clean. No mess, no evidence, no weapon a forensic team would recognize. A hand on a shoulder. A pulse. A man dead on the floor of his own lab, surrounded by the equations that got him killed.

Third time: I'm watching the notebook.

3:52. Henry shows Thorne the latest equations. Thorne leans in. Reads them.

3:53. Hand on shoulder.

He read the breakthrough. He saw how close Henry was. And he made a decision.

Sixty-one seconds. Reading to killing. Sixty-one seconds.


*


I copy the footage. Phone pointed at the security monitor, recording the playback. It's crude. A recording of a recording, grainy, timestamp visible in the corner.

It won't hold up in court.

It's not for a court.

Okay. Think it through. The Henry Taylor in my father's adopted dimension -- the one who died of a "sudden cardiac event" six months before Dad arrived -- was murdered. Same method. Same pattern. A Henry Taylor who got too close to the Bridge, too close to independence from Thorne.

And if Thorne killed Henry in that dimension, and killed Henry in this one --

How many?

The Bridge's subconscious thread surfaces an answer I didn't ask for.

Across every dimension I've visited where Thorne and Henry coexisted. Where Henry's DNA signature is present but degraded. Where the trail goes cold. The pattern holds. Henry Taylor dies. The method varies -- cardiac arrest, lab accident, sudden illness. But the timing is the same. After a breakthrough. After the research hits a critical threshold. After Henry becomes more valuable dead than alive.

Oh God.

Thorne doesn't just kill Henry Taylor. He harvests him. Lets Henry do the hard work -- years of theory, painstaking experiments, slow accumulation of knowledge. And when the equations on the whiteboard become a threat to Thorne's monopoly, he takes what he needs and eliminates the source.

Every dimension. Over and over.

My father wasn't a collaborator. He was a crop. Planted, cultivated, harvested.

I think I'm going to be sick.

I am not going to be sick. I am going to leave this building.


*


Three blocks to the alley. Hands steady. Breathing even. Eyes dry.

I'm not the woman who cried on a warehouse floor after the park. Something shifted. There's a hard, clear thing in the center of my chest now. Not grief. Not rage. Not fear.

Just: I know.

I know who Elias Thorne is. Not the charming eccentric. Not the visionary physicist. Not the guy who builds institutes and reshapes towns and smiles over scotch.

A murderer. In every dimension. Finding brilliant, trusting scientists. Using them until they get too close. Killing them with a method so clean it looks natural.

And I work for him. In my home dimension, I lead a research team at his Institute. I smile at him in the hallways. I attend his lectures. I cash his paychecks. I sit in his building and use his equipment and call him "Dr. Thorne" and I didn't know I was looking at a serial killer.

Now I do.


*


I think: home. My basement. My lab. My world.

It's 4:47 a.m. Dark except for server LEDs and the faint glow of the old pod in the corner. I built it to follow my father. Don't need it anymore. Don't need any machine. I am the machine.

Schrodinger is on the journals.

I sit on the floor next to him. He doesn't open his eye. He adjusts his purring by about a quarter-tone. His way of saying: noted.

"He killed him," I say. "Thorne killed Dad. Not our Dad. A Dad. A Henry Taylor. I watched it on a security camera, Schrodinger. Hand on his shoulder. Four seconds. Heart stopped."

The cat opens his eye.

"And the one in Dad's dimension -- where Gayelyn is -- that wasn't a heart attack either. Same method. Same deal. Thorne kills Henry Taylors across dimensions. He grows them, harvests them, and nobody ever notices because who's going to compare autopsy reports between parallel universes?"

Schrodinger sits up. Slowly. He looks at me with his one green eye.

Then he does something he almost never does.

He walks into my lap.

Not rubs against my leg. Not sits near me. Walks directly into my lap, settles, presses his face against my stomach. Full contact.

I put my hand on his back. Feel his heartbeat. Fast, steady, alive.

One cat, two realities. The only being in creation genuinely in two places at once.

And in both those places, Thorne exists.


*


My phone buzzes. My real phone, not the disposable.

Ethan.

You up?

I stare at the screen. Ethan Thorne. Son of Elias Thorne. The man who brings me tacos at unreasonable hours. The man who built the kill switch for my pod. The man who said "come back" and meant it.

I think about the park. Grocery bag. The child. The version of Ethan who was free.

I think about the footage. Hand on shoulder. Four seconds.

I type back: Yeah. Come over. Bring tacos.

He responds immediately: It's 5 a.m.

Breakfast tacos, Ethan.

On my way.

I put the phone down. Look at the cat in my lap. Look at the pod. Look at my arm, where the Bridge hums with data from twenty-nine dimensions and one murder.

Ethan is coming. Twenty minutes. He'll walk through the back door without knocking. He'll hand me a taco and sit on the camp chair and ask what's wrong, because he always sees it.

And I won't tell him.

Not about the footage. Not about the murder. Not about his father.

Not yet.

Because I don't know which way he'll go. The son who picks me, or the son who picks his father.

Schrodinger purrs in my lap.

Outside, a truck engine. Battered F-150, coming up the driveway.

I put on a face.

The back door opens.

"Lawson Marie," Ethan says. "What's going on?"

Crooked grin. Messy hair. Green eyes that see everything except the thing I'm hiding.

"Nothing," I say. "Couldn't sleep."

He hands me a taco.

I take it.


-- End of Chapter 10 --
