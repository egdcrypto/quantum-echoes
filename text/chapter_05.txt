Chapter 5

ECHOES OF BLOOD AND TIME


The tacos are good.

I want to note this for the record, because it might be the last normal thing I experience. Barbacoa. Extra cilantro. The little cup of salsa verde that Ethan always brings on the side because he knows I like to control the heat ratio.

If I'm going to die in a quantum superposition event in my own basement, at least my last thought will be about something pleasant.

It's 6:14 a.m. We're sitting on the basement stairs, eating breakfast tacos, not talking about the fact that in forty-five minutes I'm going to step inside a machine I built from scraps and try to punch a hole in reality.

Ethan is handling this by being aggressively practical.

"Checklist," he says, pulling a folded piece of paper from his back pocket. Handwritten. Of course it's handwritten. Ethan doesn't trust anything digital for important things. "Pod power: confirmed. ZPE field stability: confirmed. Resonance matrix calibrated to your father's DNA signature: confirmed. Return protocol--"

"Return protocol is theoretical," I say.

"Return protocol is theoretical," he repeats, writing it down. Underlines "theoretical" twice. "Lawson, I want to be clear about something."

"You're about to tell me this is insane."

"This is insane. But that's not what I was going to say." He folds the checklist. Puts it back in his pocket. Looks at me with those green eyes that have been looking at me since we were fourteen. "I was going to say: come back."

Two words.

"That's the plan," I say.

"It's not a plan. It's a hope. Make it a plan."

I look at him. He looks at me. Schrodinger, sitting between us on the stairs, looks at both of us and yawns.

"I'll come back," I say.

He nods. Picks up his taco. Takes a bite.

That's Ethan. Says the important thing once, means it completely, moves on to breakfast.


                                *


The pod is humming.

I've run the final diagnostics three times. Resonance matrix: locked onto the strongest of my father's DNA signatures, the one I isolated from the twenty-three pattern, the echo that burns brightest in the quantum background. ZPE field: stable. Activation sequence: loaded.

I'm wearing jeans, a t-shirt, and sneakers, because nobody told me the dress code for interdimensional travel and I figure comfort beats formality when you might be vaporized.

Schrodinger is already in the pod.

He walked in on his own. Twenty minutes ago, while I was running the third diagnostic, the cat hopped off the stairs, padded across the lab, jumped over two cable bundles with the casual athleticism of a creature who has never doubted his own physical capabilities, and settled himself on the floor of the pod like he'd been waiting for a bus.

I hadn't calibrated the resonance matrix for his mass. I hadn't planned to bring him.

But last night, standing at the bottom of the stairs, I'd felt it -- the tug, the instinct that said the cat comes with you. And now the cat had apparently reached the same conclusion independently.

So. Recalibrate. Eleven minutes. The cat waits with the patience of a being who has already decided how this is going to go.

"Okay," I say to Ethan, who's at the monitoring station -- a desk with three screens and a hardwired kill switch he insisted on installing himself. "I'm going."

"I know."

"If the readings go critical--"

"I pull the plug. I know, Lawson."

"And if I'm not back in--"

"Twenty-four hours. Then I pull the plug, call in sick to the Institute, and start figuring out how to come get you. I know." He pauses. "We went over this. Twice. Three times. I have the checklist. Go."

I step into the pod.

It's smaller than I expected. Not physically -- I built it, I know the dimensions down to the centimeter. But when you're standing inside a machine that's about to disassemble your relationship with reality, the walls feel closer.

Schrodinger presses against my ankle.

"Here we go, buddy," I whisper.

I activate the sequence.

The hum deepens. The blue light rises. The walls of my basement dissolve.

And I am somewhere else.


                                *


First thing: don't breathe.

This is deliberate. I've read my father's notes. I know the machine works -- the scorched lab proved that. But I have zero data on atmospheric conditions at the destination.

Hold breath. Take stock.

Outdoors. Sky above, ground below, wind on my face. Light is warm, golden, late afternoon. Ground is dry grass.

Okay. Test breath. Shallow.

The air smells like Texas.

Not alien. Not metallic. Not thin. Just... Texas. Dry heat. Cut grass. Faint whiff of mesquite. Lungs fill easily. No headache, no euphoria, no metallic taste.

Assessment: we're fine.

Schrodinger is already out of the pod, sniffing the ground with the focused intensity of a health inspector. Ears up. Tail relaxed. He's fine. We're fine.

Now. Where am I?

I'm standing in a field behind a strip mall. Whataburger across the parking lot. Valero gas station. Tractor Supply Company. Signage in English.

So far so good.

But the cars in the parking lot are wrong.

Not wrong as in alien. Wrong as in old. A Ford Taurus. A Chevy Cavalier. A Geo Metro, for God's sake -- a car that hasn't been manufactured since 2001.

Hmm.

I find a newspaper box outside the gas station. Metal and glass. A quarter for a paper. I don't have a quarter, but I can read the date through the glass.

March 14, 2003.

I stare at it.

2003.

I left in 2024. My father disappeared in 2008. I'm standing in 2003, five years before he vanished, twenty-one years before I stepped into the pod.

Okay.

Okay okay okay.

Think this through.

This isn't time travel. Not exactly. The newspaper is from this dimension's 2003 -- a parallel reality where time is running on a different clock. The multiverse isn't synchronized. Each dimension has its own timeline, its own "now," and the pod dropped me into one that's running twenty-one years behind mine.

Which means...

Wait.

If dimensions aren't time-synced, then my father could have landed anywhere on the timeline. Past. Present. Future. He could be in a dimension where the year is 1950 or 2080 or a date that doesn't map to anything in my calendar system.

The DNA trail doesn't just point across space. It points across time.

I sit down on the curb. Schrodinger jumps into my lap. I pet him on autopilot.

"Well," I say to the cat. "That complicates things."


                                *


The pod's instruments are limited. Crude. Designed for a tungsten ball in a controlled lab, not a woman sitting in a Whataburger parking lot having an existential crisis.

But they confirm what I suspected.

My father's DNA signature is here.

Not "in this parking lot" here. "In this dimension" here. The resonance pattern I calibrated to -- the twenty-three echo, the prime number harmonic -- it's present. Faint. Degraded. Like a footprint in sand that's been rained on.

But present.

My father was in this dimension at some point. His DNA interacted with the quantum fabric of this reality and left a trace.

But he's not here now.

Okay. So how old is the signal? Let me think about this.

The instruments aren't precise enough to give me a real number, and what does "old" even mean when the dimension's timeline isn't synced with mine? Old could mean ten years by this dimension's clock. Or a hundred years. Or last Tuesday.

The data just says: he was here. He's gone.

Dead end.

But also: proof. The DNA trail works. My father passed through this reality, and his genetic signature left a detectable echo. The theory holds. Twenty-three chromosome pairs, resonating in the quantum vacuum, traceable across dimensions.

The science works.

I can find him.

Schrodinger rubs against my shin. I'm going to interpret that as agreement.


                                *


I give myself two hours.

The air is fine. The cat is calm. The location is Earth-like and English-speaking and currently serving Whataburger, which means the fundamental constants of this universe are close enough to mine that I'm not going to sprout extra limbs.

I leave the pod in the field, hidden in tall grass. Not ideal. Best I've got.

And I walk into town.

It's surreal. Not because it's alien. Because it's not. This could be any small Texas town in 2003. The people look normal. They're wearing clothes I recognize from old photos -- low-rise jeans, chunky sneakers, a lot of denim. Someone walks past with a flip phone. A Nelly song is playing from a car radio.

I am standing in someone else's 2003, and it's so normal it makes my skin crawl.

Library. Libraries are underrated reconnaissance tools. Free, anonymous, full of information, nobody asks why you're there. I use their public computer -- Internet Explorer, dialup speed, a genuine relic of the digital Stone Age -- and I search for the things that matter.

Elias Thorne: exists. I find an article in an academic journal. A Cambridge physicist pursuing "unconventional research into quantum vacuum energy." Same man, same trajectory, same obsession. He hasn't built the Institute yet in this timeline. Still in the crackpot phase.

But he's here. Same path.

I search for Henry Taylor.

He exists too. Physicist at a university in the Midwest. Published papers on quantum entanglement. No connection to Thorne yet. In this dimension's 2003, my father and Elias Thorne haven't met.

I stare at the screen. My father's faculty photo. Younger than I've ever seen him. That lopsided grin. Hair still stubbornly present.

This isn't my father. This is a version of my father who hasn't built a State Bridge, hasn't met Thorne, hasn't vanished. This Henry Taylor still has his whole life ahead of him.

I close the browser. Leave the library. Walk back to the pod through the warm afternoon, Schrodinger trotting beside me.

I try not to think about whether this dimension's Henry Taylor will meet this dimension's Elias Thorne and make the same choices.

He will. Thorne is a constant.

But I don't know that yet.


                                *


The return jump is worse than the first.

Not physically. The pod performs identically. Smooth ZPE engagement. Same disorienting lurch of silence-then-everything.

The problem is what's in my head now.

The multiverse isn't random. It has patterns. Tendencies. Structure I'm only beginning to see. And the biggest pattern: Thorne. My dimension and this one. Same man. Same trajectory.

I materialize in my basement.

The monitors are blinking. The hum of the servers is the most beautiful sound I've ever heard, because it means I'm home. The return protocol wasn't just theoretical.

Ethan is at the monitoring station. He's been there the whole time. I can tell by the empty Dr Pepper cans around his feet.

Four of them.

Which means he's been stress-drinking Dr Pepper, which means he was more worried than he let on.

"Hi," I say.

"Hi," he says.

He doesn't rush over. Doesn't hug me. Just stands there, looking at me with something I can't fully read. Relief, sure. And something else he tucks away before I can name it.

"How long was I gone?"

"Four hours and twelve minutes."

"It felt like three hours."

"That's because you weren't watching a clock and trying not to throw up for four hours and twelve minutes."

Fair.

I step out of the pod. Schrodinger leaps out ahead of me and goes straight to the water bowl in the corner, because apparently interdimensional travel is thirsty work.

"Did it work?" Ethan asks.

"The DNA trail is real," I say. "He was there. In that dimension. Signature embedded in the quantum fabric. Faint, degraded, but traceable."

"And?"

"And there's a problem." I sit on the stairs. My legs are shakier than expected. "The dimensions aren't time-synced. I landed in 2003. Twenty-one years behind us. Dad's trail could lead to any point on the timeline in any dimension."

Ethan processes this. I watch him do it -- that thing where his eyes go distant and his jaw works and he takes a complex problem and breaks it into components like he's disassembling an engine. He's not a physicist. He's an engineer. He doesn't think about problems. He thinks about solutions.

"So you need a way to filter the signal by time," he says. "Correlate the DNA degradation with the dimensional time offset. Build a model."

I blink.

"That's... actually a really good idea."

"Don't sound so surprised."

"I'm not surprised. I'm grateful."

He looks at me. That expression again. The one he puts away. "You're welcome. Now tell me about 2003. Did they have tacos?"

"They had Whataburger."

"Close enough."


                                *


I almost miss it.

Bathroom. Washing my face. The adrenaline crash is hitting me like a truck. I'm exhausted in a way that doesn't match four hours of activity. Jumping dimensions apparently takes a toll the body keeps score of even when the clock doesn't explain it.

I look in the mirror.

And I stop.

I look exactly the same.

That shouldn't be notable. Four hours. Why would I look different? But something about it -- the specific configuration of the lines around my eyes, the exact shade of the shadows under them -- is identical to this morning.

Not similar. Identical.

Okay. Think about this.

Four hours of extreme physiological stress. Elevated cortisol. Adrenaline. The physical demands of being transported across dimensional boundaries. That should leave some trace. Puffiness. Pallor. Something.

Nothing.

Hmm.

One data point isn't a pattern. I file it.

But I start paying attention.

Over the next several jumps, each one longer, each one further along my father's trail, I check. I photograph myself before and after. I measure. I compare.

The result is consistent.

The jumps exhaust me mentally. Drain me emotionally. Push my psychological endurance to its limits.

But biologically?

Nothing changes.

I don't age during transit.

Time in other dimensions counts normally -- if I'm there a week, I age a week. But the jump itself, the crossing, the moment of superposition when I'm neither here nor there -- that time is free. Biology on pause.

Wait. Let me make sure I'm not fooling myself.

I check the photos again. Run the numbers on pixel-level skin comparison. Account for lighting differences, angle, camera artifacts.

Nope. The data holds. I don't age during transit.

I don't understand why. Not yet. The explanation will come later, and it will involve a one-eyed cat and a concept that will strain my definition of science until it breaks.

For now, I just know: the multiverse is lending me time.


                                *


I jump again the next week. And the week after. And the week after that.

Each jump follows my father's DNA trail. I've refined the calibration, narrowed the signal, learned to prioritize stronger echoes over weaker ones. Ethan helps me build a better resonance filter from parts he salvages from the Institute's surplus inventory.

The Institute is Elias Thorne's kingdom. We're using his parts to build a machine to find a man who may have been running from him.

We don't talk about that.

We don't talk about a lot of things.

Each jump teaches me something.

Dimension two (mine, not Dad's numbering): coastal city, breathable, temperate. Dad's signature present but faint. Dead end. Duration: six hours. I buy water at a convenience store with cash from home. The cashier looks at my twenty-dollar bill funny. Andrew Jackson's portrait is on the wrong side. I leave quickly.

Dimension three: rural landscape. Breathable but thick -- high humidity, like breathing through a wet cloth. Dad's signature stronger here. More recent. But he's gone. I find a farmhouse. The people speak English with an accent I can't place. They're friendly. They offer food. I eat it and don't explain.

Dimension four: a big city. Breathable, modern, bustling. Newspapers say 2031. Seven years ahead of home.

And the Thorne Institute exists here too.

Bigger. Sleeker. A hundred-acre campus. The signage says Thorne Global Research Initiative. Not just an institute. A global initiative.

This dimension's Thorne isn't just a physicist. He's an industry. Defense contracts. Government partnerships. Private research army.

Different dimension. Bigger Thorne. Same man.

I'm starting to see the pattern.


                                *


Dimensions five, six, seven, eight.

Every reality I visit, Thorne is there. Not in person -- I'm careful to avoid his institutions. But his name surfaces in every dimension like a recurring signal in a data set. Sometimes small: a grant, a faculty position, a paper. Sometimes massive: a corporation, a government contractor, a name that reshapes communities.

The scale changes. The man doesn't.

I log it. I don't have a framework yet. Just a growing body of evidence that Elias Thorne is something more than a man who exists in multiple dimensions. He's a pattern. A recurring variable that always resolves to the same value.

I think about Dad's note. It's not you, it's not me, it's Schrodinger.

The cat in the box. The observer and the observed. The wave function that collapses into a single state.

Is Thorne a collapsed wave function? A man who exists in the same state in every reality? No superposition. No uncertainty. Always the same.

Wait. That doesn't work. A truly collapsed wave function would be deterministic, but he clearly makes different choices in different dimensions -- the scale changes, the methods change. He's not identical everywhere. He's... convergent. Different paths, same destination.

That's not a collapsed wave function. That's something else.

I don't know what.

More data needed.


                                *


Between jumps, I live my other life.

The Institute. Dr. Lawson Taylor, team lead, string theory research. Mentoring junior physicists. Giving talks at conferences. Wearing blazers.

Normal. Professional. A woman with a career and colleagues who think she goes home and watches TV.

She doesn't. She goes home and calibrates a quantum transportation device in her basement.

The dual life is exhausting. Not because of the deception -- I'm actually good at deception, which is a skill I never wanted and can't put on a resume. Because of the cognitive dissonance. At the Institute, I think in theories. In the basement, I think in realities. At the Institute, the multiverse is a mathematical abstraction. In the basement, the multiverse is a Whataburger parking lot in 2003.

And the whole time, I'm working at the Thorne Institute. Under Thorne's roof. Funded by Thorne's money. Smiling at colleagues with Thorne's name on their paychecks.

The man whose name I keep finding in every dimension.

The man who funded my father's research.

I sit in meetings and nod and take notes and think: you're in every reality I've visited. Every single one. Why?

But I smile. I nod. I play the part.

And at night, in the basement, Schrodinger on the journals, Ethan in the camp chair, the pod humming -- at night I'm real.


                                *


Nine jumps in. The trail is cold but consistent.

My father was in every dimension I've visited. His DNA echo is faint but present in each one. A quantum fingerprint pressed into each reality. He moved through these places. Breathed this air. Stood where I'm standing.

And he kept going.

That's the part that gets me. He didn't stop. Didn't stay. Didn't find what he was looking for -- or didn't find it yet -- and kept jumping. Kept rolling the dice. Kept moving through the multiverse with a pod and a cat and whatever he could scavenge.

No return protocol. No monitoring station. No Ethan with a checklist and a kill switch and a look on his face that says more than he'll ever actually say out loud. Just a man and a cat. Alone. Holding their breath before every jump.

I'm following his trail.

But I'm also following his loneliness. I can feel it in the spaces between the data points. The way his signature is always moving, never settling. He was running. Or searching. Or both.

Where were you going, Dad?

What were you looking for?

Did you find it?

The pod hums. The cat sleeps. And somewhere in the multiverse, in a dimension I haven't reached yet, my father's trail is waiting.
