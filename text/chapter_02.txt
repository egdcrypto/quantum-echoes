CHAPTER 2 - THE VANISHING


The morning of the day I disappear, I leave my daughter a note in a book.

This is not unusual. I leave notes everywhere. Have for years. In Lawson's backpack, between the pages of her textbooks, tucked into the pocket of her lab coat -- the one with the coffee stain on the left sleeve that she won't wash because it has "character." I put them in her shoes sometimes. She says this is annoying. She has kept every single one.

Today's note is different.

I'm standing in the kitchen. 6:47 a.m. Lawson is asleep upstairs. She has class at ten but she won't get up until nine-fifteen because she is physically incapable of being early for anything that doesn't involve a physics problem. She gets this from her mother, who was late to our first date by forty minutes and to our wedding by twelve.

God, I loved that woman.

Schrodinger is sitting on the kitchen table. He's not supposed to be on the table.

"Off," I say.

He looks at me. He has heard this word before. He is not persuaded.

"Fine. Stay."

He was going to anyway.

I open the book. Temmer's Quantum Mechanics, third edition. Same copy since grad school. Coffee rings, three colors of margin notes, spine cracked at the superposition chapter. I find pages 142 and 143. I tear a piece from the margin of 144 -- carefully, because even right now I can't bring myself to rip a textbook without precision. Some things are sacred.

I write nine words.

Lawson, it's not you, it's not me, it's Schrodinger.

My hand is steady. This surprises me. I am about to do something that will either validate twenty years of work or kill me in a way nobody will be able to explain, and my hand is not shaking.

Huh.

I tuck the note between the pages. Close the book. Put it on the shelf in my home office, third row, between Feynman's Lectures and a dog-eared copy of Cosmos that Lawson gave me for Christmas when she was twelve. She'd written in the front cover: Dad -- the universe is weird and so are we. Love, your favorite quantum state.

Best thing anyone's ever written to me. And yes, I'm including a very nice peer review I once got from Stephen Hawking's research group.

I pour a second cup of coffee. I make one for Lawson too -- way too much sugar, not enough cream, the way she likes it -- and leave it on the counter with a Post-it: Your old man loves you. Don't forget to eat something that isn't caffeine.

Then I pick up the cat.

This is a process. If you've never tried to pick up a cat who does not want to be picked up, let me walk you through it. Step one: approach casually. Do not make eye contact. Do not telegraph intent. If the cat senses that you want to pick him up, it's over. Step two: one hand under the belly, other hand supporting the hind legs. Step three: lift with an expression of complete nonchalance, as if this is something that is simply happening and nobody planned it. Step four: accept the look of betrayal. Step five: proceed to car.

I've been doing this for seven years. I have it down to about four seconds.

I drive to the lab.


*


The Thorne Institute for Advanced Physics sits on a hundred acres of cleared Texas scrubland. Glass and steel buildings that look like someone wanted to build a spaceship and got talked down to a research campus at the last minute. It's impressive in the way expensive things are impressive.

Elias Thorne built it. Buildings, research programs, faculty -- all of it. He's been at this for decades. Patient, intentional, thinking in timelines that make everyone else look like they're planning lunch. I've known him for fifteen years, since he came up to me after a conference talk about quantum vacuum energy that the rest of the audience had politely slept through. Elias didn't sleep through it. He sat in the front row, leaned forward for the entire forty-five minutes, and afterward bought me a scotch and said, "You're right about all of it, and I can prove it."

He couldn't prove it. But he could fund it. And for a theoretical physicist whose theory nobody took seriously and who had a daughter to raise on an academic salary, funding was enough.

I trust Elias. I want to say that clearly. What I'm about to do is built on that trust. I've never examined it too closely. It's like the foundation of a house you've lived in for years. It holds. Why would you check?

The cat sits in the passenger seat, watching Texas go by.

"Big day, buddy," I say.

He looks at me. One green eye.

"If this works, everything changes. If it doesn't --"

I don't finish. There isn't really a good way to finish that sentence when your daughter is asleep at home and there's a coffee going cold on the counter next to a Post-it that might be the last normal thing you ever said to her.

He turns back to the window. Fair enough.


*


The State Bridge is ready.

Seven years of building. Longer if you count the theory, which I do, because theory is just practice without a budget.

The machine fills an entire room in the Institute's basement. Massive electromagnetic coils. A resonance chamber lined with superconducting material that Thorne sourced from contacts I've learned not to ask about. A control system I designed that Thorne's engineers built to specs so precise that three of them quit during the process. One of them cited "unreasonable expectations of physics" in his resignation letter. I had it framed.

Okay. The theory. Let me try to explain this.

So. Reality. It's held together by the zero-point energy field. The quantum vacuum. The background hum of everything. Most physicists treat it as noise -- just the universe doing its thing, irrelevant, uninteresting.

I've spent twenty years saying it's a signal.

And here's what happens when you take that seriously. The ZPE field doesn't just fill space. It connects spaces. Different dimensions. Parallel versions of reality, layered on top of each other. Think of it like... okay, think of radio stations. You're on one frequency, and right next to you, on a slightly different frequency, there's a whole other station playing a whole other song. Same space. Different channel.

The State Bridge changes the channel.

That's it. That's the whole idea. You tune the field, you shift the frequency, you step from one reality into the next.

I know how it sounds. I've been living inside this idea so long that I sometimes forget it sounds completely insane to normal people.

Today we test it.

Elias is in the control room. He insisted on being here -- not just watching, but running the controls himself. His hands are steady on the console. He has that look. I've seen it for fifteen years. The look of a man who wants the future so badly he can taste it. Same look he had over scotch. Same look when he talks about the Institute, his vision, what zero-point energy could mean.

I used to find it inspiring. Lately I find it... intense. The kind of focus that doesn't have room for doubt. Which is either the mark of a visionary or --

I don't finish the thought. Not today.

"Henry." His voice over the intercom. Clipped, precise, faint British accent that shows up when he's excited. "Systems are nominal. Field is stable."

"Copy that."

I'm standing in the resonance chamber. The cat is in my arms.

This was not the plan. The plan was a tungsten test sphere. Known mass, known quantum properties, nice and controlled. But Schrodinger walked into the chamber while I was doing final checks. Walked right in, hopped up on the main coil because it was warm, and settled himself down with the determined purposefulness of a cat who has found a good spot and will not be moved.

And I felt something. Like the start of a thought I hadn't finished.

I picked him up. I didn't put him down.

"Field strength at seventy percent and climbing," Elias says. "You should step out now, Henry. The sphere is loaded."

"Change of plan," I say.

Silence.

"Henry?"

"I'm going, Elias. Not the sphere. Me."

More silence. I can hear it stretch across the intercom.

"That's not what we discussed."

"No."

"The risks --"

"I know the risks." I do. Twenty years of them. I've run the models, checked the math, run the simulations until I could recite them asleep. And some nights I do. The probability of successful transit is high. The probability of return is... less certain.

But there's a note in a book on a shelf in my home office. And there's a cat in my arms whose purring is so steady it's almost like he knows something I don't. And I am a man who has spent his whole career listening to the universe hum and believing it was trying to tell him something.

Maybe I'm wrong. But I'm not sending a tungsten ball in my place.

"Henry, I have to recommend --"

"I know." I cut him off gently. The way you stop a friend from saying the reasonable thing when you're past the point of reason. "I know what you're going to say. And I appreciate it. But I'm going."

Schrodinger shifts in my arms. Purring against my chest.

"Hit the button, Elias."

A pause. Three seconds. Four.

"Initiating," he says.

The hum begins. Not the usual lab noise. Something deeper. Something that lives in the gaps between particles, between heartbeats. The zero-point field, amplified, directed by seven years of engineering and twenty years of theory and a man holding a cat who is too stubborn to send a tungsten ball instead.

The light comes. Blue, then white, then something past white. A color I don't have a word for. A frequency that shouldn't exist here because it doesn't belong here.

I think of Lawson.

I think of the note.

I think of Gayelyn.

I think: I hope she's angry. I hope she's angry enough to find that note and refuse to let it be the end. I hope she figures it out. She will. She's smarter than me. She's always been smarter than me.

The light swallows everything.

The last thing I hear is the cat purring.
