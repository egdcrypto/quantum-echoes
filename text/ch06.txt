Chapter 5

THE RULES


Dimension forty-one.

I am in a jail cell.

Let me explain how a physicist with two doctorates ends up behind bars in a reality that isn't his own. It's a short story. It goes like this: hunger makes you stupid.

The dimension is Earth-like. Aggressively Earth-like. Strip mall. Parking lot. A grocery store with a name I don't recognize -- FreshWay -- but otherwise indistinguishable from any Kroger in Central Texas. Same fluorescent lighting. Same produce misting system. Same muzak playing something that was popular ten years ago.

Schrodinger is in the pod, hidden in a drainage culvert behind an abandoned gas station about half a mile from town. I've got the go-bag. I look rough -- three weeks without a real shower will do that -- but passable. Close enough to "guy having a bad month" that nobody looks twice.

The problem: I have no money. Not no money as in "low on funds." No money as in the currency here has a woman's face I don't recognize on the twenty and the bills are slightly wider than the ones in my wallet from six dimensions ago. My cash is wallpaper. My credit cards are plastic rectangles with numbers that correspond to no account in any bank in this reality.

I am broke in the most absolute sense possible.

And I'm out of food. The last can from the empty town went two dimensions ago. I've been running on stream water and something that might have been a pear from dimension thirty-eight. My body is sending memos. Strongly worded ones.

So I steal.

Not well. Not like a professional. Like a hungry physicist who has never stolen anything in his life. I put a loaf of bread under my jacket. Canned soup in my cargo pockets. A bag of jerky -- the good kind, the thick-cut stuff -- tucked into the waistband of my jeans, which is uncomfortable and suspicious and, in hindsight, the detail that gets me caught.

The security guard is a kid. Maybe twenty-two. He's polite about it.

"Sir, I'm going to need you to come with me."

The jerky has slipped and is now visibly protruding from my waistband like the world's least convincing concealed weapon.

"I can explain."

I cannot explain.


                                *


The police are professional and bored. Small town. Not a lot of crime. A middle-aged shoplifter is the most exciting thing that's happened this week.

They run my fingerprints.

Nothing.

They run them again.

Nothing.

A detective comes in. Older. Tired eyes. The kind of cop who's seen enough to know when something doesn't add up.

"No prints in the system. No ID. No driver's license, no social security number, no record of a Henry Taylor matching your description in any database we can access." A pause. "You want to tell me who you are?"

"My name is Henry Taylor. I'm a physics professor."

"Where?"

"I -- I'm between positions."

"Between positions." Writing it down. "And where do you live, Professor Taylor?"

Here's the thing about lying. I'm terrible at it. Always have been. Gayelyn used to say I had the world's most transparent face. Lawson inherited her mother's poker expression. I inherited my father's tendency to look guilty even when I haven't done anything.

But desperation is a teacher.

"I don't remember," I say.

He looks up.

"I was in an accident. A few weeks ago. I've been -- confused. Disoriented. I woke up in a field outside town with no wallet, no phone, no memory of how I got here."

"An accident."

"I think so. I have gaps. In my memory." I touch the side of my head. There's actually a bruise there from bumping it on the pod's doorframe two jumps ago. "I'm a professor. I know that. My name is Henry. I know that. But the rest is -- fragments."

He studies me. I can see the calculation. On one hand: clearly not a professional criminal. Stole bread and soup and jerky, not jewelry. Fingerprints genuinely not in any system. On the other hand: something is very wrong with this picture.

"We're going to hold you," he says. "I'm going to make some calls."


                                *


Three days.

The cell is clean. The food is better than what I've been eating. There's a mattress and a blanket and a toilet and a small window that faces a parking lot.

I should be grateful for the rest. I'm not.

Because the pod is in a drainage culvert half a mile away. With Schrodinger inside. And every hour I'm in this cell is an hour someone could find it. A kid exploring. A city worker. A dog walker whose retriever has a good nose and a bad sense of boundaries.

If they find the pod, I'm done. Not arrested-done. Stranded-done. No pod means no Bridge. No Bridge means no jumping. I stay here. In this dimension. Forever. A man with no identity, no history, no existence in any record on this Earth.

That thought keeps me awake all three nights.

Also keeping me awake: Schrodinger. He has food -- I always leave emergency rations in the pod now. Water too. But he's alone. In a box. In a culvert.

The irony is not lost on me. A cat named Schrodinger, sealed in a box, whose state I cannot observe.

Day two. The detective comes back. He's made his calls. Nobody matching my description has been reported missing. No hospital has records of a head trauma patient who walked out. I'm a ghost.

"I'm going to be honest with you, Henry." First-name basis now. Pity, not suspicion. "We can't hold you much longer on a shoplifting charge. Especially with the..." He gestures vaguely at his own head. "But I can't just release you onto the street."

"What are my options?"

"County hospital has a psychiatric evaluation unit. I can have you transferred there. They'll assess your memory issues, get you into the system."

"That sounds good," I say, because it's what a confused amnesiac would say.

What I'm thinking: the hospital will have shift changes. Entry points. Ways out that don't involve locked doors and fingerprint scanners. A hospital is designed to keep people alive. A jail is designed to keep people in. These are different engineering problems.

Day three. Transfer paperwork goes through. I'm driven to the county hospital in the back of a patrol car. The officer is kind. Makes small talk about the weather, the high school football team.

I nod along. I am a physics professor from a parallel dimension pretending to have amnesia while worrying about a cat trapped in a quantum machine. Normal Tuesday.

They process me into the psych evaluation unit at 3:47 p.m. I note the shift change schedule posted on the nurse's station wall. Night shift starts at 11:00 p.m. There will be a gap. There's always a gap.

At 11:22 p.m., during the handoff confusion, I walk out through a service entrance next to the loading dock. Down two flights of stairs. Through a hallway that smells like industrial cleaner. Out a door with a sign that says AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY, which I am not, but the door opens from the inside without a badge.

I run.

Not dramatically. Purposefully. A middle-aged man jogging through a parking lot in hospital-issued scrubs. I keep the pace even, the posture neutral. Not fleeing. Just exercising. At eleven at night. In hospital clothes.

I reach the culvert twenty-eight minutes later. The pod is there. Untouched. Schrodinger is sitting on top of it, because of course he got out -- the latch has never been cat-proof -- and he regards me with one-eyed irritation that communicates volumes.

Where. Have. You. Been.

"Jail," I tell him. "Then a psychiatric ward. Long story."

I get in the pod. Schrodinger follows. I activate the jump sequence before anyone can notice that the amnesia patient has vanished.

New rule on the wall:

7. Always pay when you can. Steal only what you need.

Underneath, smaller:

8. Never get caught without a story.


                                *


The rules are becoming a manual.

I didn't plan this. I don't sit down and compose them. They arrive the way scar tissue arrives -- as the body's response to damage. Something goes wrong. I survive it. The lesson goes on the wall.

By dimension forty-five:

9. Learn the money before you spend it. Watch what other people use.
10. Hide the pod FIRST. Explore SECOND.

By dimension fifty:

11. Three words in the local language: hello, sorry, thank you. Learn them before you need them.

The Sharpie is running low. I find a replacement in an office supply store in dimension forty-seven. Pay for it, actually pay for it, with a coin I pick up off a sidewalk. The most honest transaction in the history of interdimensional commerce.


                                *


Dimension fifty-five.

This one takes me a while to figure out.

The air is fine. The buildings are normal. Cars on the road. People on the sidewalks. English on the signs, though the accent is slightly different -- clipped, precise, almost British but not quite.

I walk into what looks like a city center. Shops, restaurants, a park with a fountain. Normal. Comfortable. After weeks of alien caves and empty towns and jail cells, I'm almost relaxed.

Then I notice.

Every shop owner is a woman. Every police officer is a woman. Every person in a suit walking with purpose is a woman. The men are there -- pushing strollers, carrying shopping bags, standing slightly behind their partners in conversation.

I watch for twenty minutes from a park bench. A man approaches a woman at a crosswalk. He waits for her to acknowledge him before speaking. She checks her phone while he talks. Nods without looking up. Walks away.

He stands there for a moment. Then adjusts his expression. Moves on.

Oh.

Oh, I see.

I walk into a bookstore. The shelves are organized normally, except the "Women's Interest" section is full of business strategy, political biography, and hard science. The "Men's Interest" section has cookbooks, parenting guides, and something called "Finding Your Purpose Beyond the Home."

I pick up a physics journal from the magazine rack. The editorial board: twelve women, two men. The lead article is by someone named Dr. Patricia Chen. The contributor photos show women in lab coats. The one man pictured is listed as "research assistant."

I am fascinated.

And I'm about to make a series of mistakes.

Mistake one: I walk up to the bookstore counter and ask the man working there if they have anything on zero-point energy research.

He stares at me. "You mean in the physics section?"

"Yes."

"That's..." He glances around. Lowers his voice. "That's not really our section. You might try the university bookstore."

The way he says it. Like I've asked for something embarrassing. Like a man browsing the physics section is transgressive somehow.

Mistake two: I go to a coffee shop. Order a coffee. The barista -- a woman -- takes my order without looking at me. Normal enough. But when I sit at a table near the window and pull out my atmospheric sensor to take readings, the woman at the next table leans over.

"Excuse me. Is that yours?"

"The sensor? Yes."

"Did you build that?"

"I did, actually. It measures--"

"That's very clever." A smile. Patronizing. The exact smile I've seen on the faces of senior physicists talking to first-year grad students. "My husband tinkers too. He made the most amazing spice rack last year."

She compares my atmospheric spectrometer to a spice rack. And she means it as a compliment.

Mistake three: I try to visit the university. I walk up to the physics building -- it exists, it's labeled, I can see equipment through the windows. A woman in a lab coat stops me at the entrance.

"Can I help you?"

"I'm a physicist. I was hoping to--"

"A physicist." The same tone you'd use if someone told you they were a unicorn veterinarian. "Do you have an appointment? Are you someone's assistant?"

"No, I'm--"

"This is a research facility. I can't just let people wander in. You understand." She's already turning away. "If you want to schedule a tour, speak to reception."

I stand there for a moment.

This feeling.

This specific feeling -- of being looked through, talked over, assumed to be less than what you are because of something you can't control. This tight, hot thing in my chest that's part anger and part something worse. Confusion. The genuine confusion of being treated as if your expertise doesn't count because of the body it lives in.

And I think about Lawson.

I think about every conference where she presented data and the questions went to me. Every meeting where she was introduced as "Henry Taylor's daughter" instead of "Dr. Taylor." Every time she said something brilliant and the room heard it as surprising.

"Can you believe she figured that out?"

Can you believe.

She.

I sit on a bench outside the physics building for a long time. Schrodinger is in my jacket -- I've started carrying him in the inner pocket for dimensions where a loose cat attracts attention. He pokes his head out and surveys the campus.

"This is what it feels like," I tell him. "This is what it's like for her."

He blinks.

"I had no idea."

He has no opinion on this. He's a cat. Gender politics don't apply to cats. Cats operate on a binary of "feeds me" and "doesn't feed me," which, now that I think about it, is a more equitable system than anything humans have managed.

One more thing I notice, and this one matters for later: in this dimension, there's a male version of Lawson.

I see his name on a university bulletin board. Dr. Lawrence Taylor. Same field -- quantum physics, ZPE theory. Same last name. And when I find a faculty photo, the resemblance is unmistakable. Same jawline. Same intensity in the eyes. Different chromosome arrangement, same person.

Male Lawson. In a dimension where men are dismissed from physics the way women are dismissed in mine.

I think about what that means. If Lawson exists as a man in some dimensions, then gender isn't fixed. It varies. Some dimensions flip it entirely. Some partially.

This is going to matter. I don't know how yet. But I write it down.

12. Gender flips. Some dimensions reverse everything. Lawson is male in some realities.

Not exactly a survival rule. More of a note. The wall is becoming a journal as much as a manual.


                                *


Dimension sixty-five.

Silence.

Not the empty-town silence from dimension four, which was absence. This is presence. There are people everywhere -- a city, dense, active, alive. But nobody is talking.

I step out of the pod. I've hidden it in a narrow alley between two buildings. The air is fine. Gravity is fine. Temperature is fine. The atmospheric sensor gives me green across the board.

But the city is soundless in a way that makes no sense. I can hear ambient noise -- traffic, wind, footsteps, the hum of machinery. What I can't hear is a single human voice.

People pass me on the sidewalk. They glance at me. At each other. I watch two women meet at a corner. They smile. Their expressions shift -- a sequence of micro-expressions, too fast and too precise to be normal conversation. Something is being communicated. I just can't hear it.

Then I feel it.

Pressure. At the edges of my mind. Like someone pressing a thumb against the outside of a balloon. Not painful. Not unpleasant. Just -- there. A presence. An attention.

I stop walking. A man passing me stops too. Looks at me. His eyebrows draw together. Concentration. The pressure intensifies.

He's trying to talk to me.

With his mind.

"I can't--" I start, and the sound of my own voice is obscene in this silence. Everyone within earshot turns. Stares. The man in front of me flinches. Like I've shouted in a library. Like I've done something rude.

I shut my mouth. Stand there. The man studies me. The pressure at my temples shifts -- not words, not images, just a kind of emotional texture. Curiosity. Then concern. Then something like pity.

He understands. I can feel that he understands. He knows I'm broken. Not "different." Broken. In the same way you'd look at an adult who can't read. Not with contempt. With a gentle, devastating kindness that's somehow worse.

I'm functionally deaf and mute. In a world where every human being communicates through a neural frequency I can't access.

Schrodinger, I should note, is fine. The cat sits on my shoulder and radiates the feline equivalent of mild interest. The telepaths notice him. Several smile. One woman reaches up and touches his head, and I feel a burst of warmth that I'm fairly sure isn't mine.

They can feel the cat. The cat might be able to feel them. I can't feel anything except the thumb on the balloon.


                                *


I stay in the telepathic dimension for four days. Partly because the air is good and the people are kind. Partly because I'm a scientist and this is the most extraordinary thing I've encountered in sixty-five dimensions.

They develop a system for communicating with me. Gestures, mostly. Pointing. Facial expressions exaggerated for my benefit, the way you might speak loudly and slowly to someone who doesn't share your language. It's humiliating and necessary.

On day two, an older woman -- silver-haired, calm expression, radiating an authority I can feel even through my broken reception -- brings me a device.

Small. Fits in my palm. A smooth oval of dark material, warm to the touch, with a single recessed contact point on the underside. She mimes placing it against the base of my skull.

I hesitate.

She waits. Patient. The pressure at my temples shifts to something I interpret as: safe. Trust. Gift.

I place the device against my neck. It adheres on its own. Slight vibration. Then --

Not telepathy. Nothing that clean. But the emotional impressions sharpen. The thumb on the balloon becomes a hand. I can feel textures in the pressure. The woman's calm authority resolves into something more specific: a teacher's patience. She has done this before. For others like me. Visitors. People without the gift.

The device doesn't give me telepathy. It's a crutch. A hearing aid for a sense I don't possess. It translates intent into rough emotional shapes -- the way a colorblind person might see the world through tinted glasses. Not the real thing. An approximation.

But it's enough to function.

Over the next two days, I learn: the pressure has direction. Each person is a point source. The emotional textures vary by individual -- some are warm, some are sharp, some are so complex I can't parse them. Children broadcast louder than adults, with less control. The elderly are precise, modulated, almost musical.

One observation I don't know what to do with: the neural field has a quality I recognize. Not the content -- I can't read telepathic content. The texture. The way it feels against my mind. It reminds me of the hum. Same quality. Different bandwidth. As if the hum and the telepathic field are two expressions of the same underlying medium, the way infrared and visible light are different frequencies on the same spectrum.

I note it. I don't pursue it. I have bigger problems than metaphysics.

And the device itself is remarkable. I examine it at night in the pod, using the HUD's diagnostic tools. The construction is beyond anything in my home dimension. Biocompatible material that interfaces with the nervous system through the skin. No implant. No surgery. Just contact and it reads neural output.

Crude, compared to what the telepaths can do natively. But the engineering principle is sound: machine-to-neural-interface communication is possible. Direct. Without wires, without electrodes, without any of the clunky apparatus we use back home.

I think about this for a long time.

If this device can translate intent from brain to machine, the reverse should work too. Machine to brain. Which means a navigation system that interfaces directly with the operator's nervous system. A ZPE field sensor that feeds data not to a screen but to your mind.

A wearable Bridge.

I don't have the engineering skill to build that. But Lawson does. If she ever gets her hands on this device...

I keep it. Obviously.

13. Keep everything useful. You never know which piece solves the puzzle.


                                *


The scavenging accelerates.

By dimension seventy, the pod looks less like a phone booth and more like a pawn shop. Every surface that isn't a critical system has something strapped to it, clipped to it, or shoved into a crevice.

But certain acquisitions matter more than others.

Dimension fifty-two: I find an atmospheric sensor that makes my jury-rigged version look like a toy. Compact, about the size of a deck of cards. Real-time readout of O2, CO2, nitrogen, methane, and about forty toxins I can't pronounce. Made by a civilization whose material science is decades ahead of mine. I trade my wristwatch for it. The seller -- a woman in an open-air tech market -- gives me a look that says I'm getting the worse end of this deal.

She's wrong. That sensor has saved my life three times already.

Dimension sixty-one: medical scanner. This one is a story.

I'm in an advanced Earth-like dimension. Clean city, quiet streets, technology embedded in everything. The buildings have a seamless quality -- no visible wiring, no exposed infrastructure. Like the technology grew rather than was built.

I find a medical clinic. Walk-in. No appointment needed. The intake process is automated -- a kiosk that scans you and assigns you to a provider. I go through it because I'm curious and because I haven't seen a doctor in over a year of subjective time.

The kiosk gives me a full readout. Vitals, blood chemistry, organ function, cellular health markers.

And then it gives me something I'm not expecting.

Cellular age: 43.2 years.

I stare at the number.

My birthday is March 15, 1965. I left my home dimension in 2008. I was 43 years old.

I've been jumping for -- what? Months? The HUD says approximately fourteen months of total elapsed subjective time. Fourteen months of eating irregularly, sleeping badly, breathing alien atmospheres, falling down ravines, sitting in jail cells. Fourteen months of hard physical use.

Cellular age: 43.2 years.

Not forty-four. Not forty-four and change. Forty-three point two. Basically unchanged.

I know what my body should look like after fourteen months of this. I've seen it in the mirror -- the same face, the same hands, the same gray at the temples that was there when I left. I've noticed. Of course I've noticed. But noticing isn't data. Noticing is impression. Bias. The unreliable narrator of my own experience.

This is data. Cold, clinical, unambiguous data.

I am not aging.

I ask the kiosk for a handheld scanner. It dispenses one from a slot -- apparently this dimension's version of a take-home monitor. Sleek, palm-sized, with a display that shows vitals, blood composition, and cellular age markers.

I take it. I don't feel guilty. I'm a man who hasn't aged in over a year. The universe owes me a medical scanner.

Back in the pod, I scan Schrodinger. He allows this with the resignation of a cat who has accepted that his human is insane.

Cellular age: 7.1 years.

He was seven when we left. Seven years, maybe a month.

Fourteen months of dimension-hopping. The cat hasn't aged either.

I sit in the pod and look at the numbers. Then I look at the cat. The cat looks at me.

"It's you," I say. "Something about you. About the superposition. About the fact that there are two of you."

He licks his paw.

"Very helpful. Thank you."

I don't have the framework for this yet. But the data is clear: whatever is happening to time -- or to our biology within time -- it's linked to the transit. To the superposition state. To the cat who exists in two places at once and doesn't know it.

I log everything. I scan myself weekly from this point on. The numbers don't move. Forty-three. Always forty-three.


                                *


Dimension sixty-nine: high-density energy cell. Found it in a junk shop of all places, in a dimension where the dominant species had moved past portable power storage. What they considered obsolete was decades beyond the best lithium-ion technology back home. About the size of a soda can. Holds a charge that supplemented the ZPE harvester for weeks.

This matters because some dimensions have weak ZPE fields. The harvester drops to 1.5 kW, 1.2 kW, sometimes barely enough to keep the pod's systems alive. In those dimensions, the energy cell is the difference between jumping and being stranded.

14. Redundant power. Always.

Dimension seventy-three: the partial map.

This one changes everything.

I'm in a dimension with advanced physics. Not just ahead of mine -- differently advanced. They've taken a different path through the same territory. Their ZPE research isn't focused on energy harvesting or dimensional travel. It's focused on cartography.

They're mapping the multiverse.

I find the database in a research facility's public access terminal. Not classified -- published. Open. They don't consider it secret, the same way we don't consider a road atlas classified information.

The map is incomplete. It covers maybe forty dimensions in a local cluster -- their neighborhood of the multiverse, if you will. Each dimension is represented as a node with harmonic coordinates. The connections between them are mapped by ZPE field resonance -- which dimensions are "adjacent" in the frequency spectrum, which are distant, which are unreachable.

And the navigation math is there. How to set a resonance array to target a specific harmonic frequency. How to calculate the energy requirements for a given jump distance. How to predict atmospheric conditions from ZPE field harmonics.

Everything I've been trying to figure out by trial and error. Written down. Published. Peer-reviewed.

I spend six hours at that terminal copying everything I can into the HUD's data storage. The format doesn't translate perfectly -- their notation system is different from mine -- but the underlying math is universal. Numbers are numbers. Primes are primes.

That night, in the pod, I convert their coordinate system to mine. It takes three hours and two false starts. Schrodinger sleeps through the entire thing, which is his way of peer-reviewing my math.

When I'm done, I have a map.

Not a complete one. Forty dimensions out of who knows how many. But forty is more than zero. Forty means I'm not rolling dice anymore. I can look at the harmonic profile of a target and make an educated guess about what I'll find there.

Strong ZPE harmonics in the lower primes? Probably stable, possibly Earth-like. Weak harmonics? Volatile. Dominant harmonics in the high primes? Exotic. Alien. Unpredictable.

It's not GPS. It's more like a sailor's chart from the fifteenth century -- rough coastlines, approximate distances, "here be dragons" notations. But it means I can choose. For the first time since the pod fired and dropped me in an alien cave, I have a say in where I go.

I sit with the map for a long time.

"Okay," I say to the cat. "We're not lost anymore. We're just far from home."

He opens one eye. Closes it. Judged and found adequate.


                                *


Dimension eighty-two.

I need to talk about this one.

The map has been good to me. Twelve jumps since I acquired it, and every one has landed in a breathable dimension. Some more hospitable than others, but nothing that tried to kill me in the first ninety seconds. This is a radical improvement.

Dimension eighty-two is Earth-like. Very Earth-like. The closest to home I've found since the almost-homes started wrecking me. Same-model cars on the road. Same fast-food chains on the highway. Same language, same accent, same everything.

I'm walking through a small city. Downtown. Brick buildings, local shops, a courthouse with a copper dome going green. Saturday afternoon. People out. Normal life. I've got coffee. Schrodinger is in the pod, which is parked behind a Goodwill. The atmospheric sensor reads textbook Earth. The map's harmonics for this dimension are almost identical to my home baseline.

Almost home.

I turn a corner onto a side street. There's a man walking toward me. About thirty yards away. He's got coffee too. Looking at his phone.

I notice three things simultaneously.

His walk. The slight hitch in the left stride from a basketball injury in college.

His build. Six-one, narrow shoulders, the posture of someone who spent too many years hunched over a lab bench.

His face.

My face.

The headache hits like a bullet. Not a gradual onset. Not a building pressure. An explosion behind my eyes, instant and total, the worst pain I've ever felt and I've broken ribs. My vision whites out. When it comes back, there are two of everything -- no, three. For one sickening, reality-bending instant, I see the street from three perspectives simultaneously: mine, his, and one from somewhere above, as if a third version of me is watching from a rooftop that doesn't exist.

Three Henry Taylors. Three quantum signatures. Same DNA, same prime resonance, same chord played on the same twenty-three keys.

Occupying the same fifty-yard stretch of sidewalk.

The universe does not like this.

The streetlight above me flickers. Not the normal flicker of a bad bulb. A strobe. On-off-on-off, cycling faster than the electrical system should allow. A car alarm erupts from a sedan parked at the curb. Then another. Then a third.

My nose is bleeding. I can feel it -- hot and sudden, running over my lip.

The other Henry has stopped walking. He's staring at me. His phone is lowered. His expression cycles through confusion, recognition, horror. He touches his temple. Winces. He feels it too.

He doesn't know what he's feeling. He's just a man walking to get coffee on a Saturday and now his head is splitting and there's a stranger across the street who looks exactly like him.

I need to leave.

I need to leave RIGHT NOW.

But my legs aren't cooperating. The pain is doing something to my motor control. I take a step and the world tilts. The three-perspective vision pulses again -- I can see myself from his eyes, stumbling, nose bleeding, eyes wide.

The shop window to my right cracks. A single fracture line, running from bottom to top, the glass stress-fracturing from some invisible force. The parking meter on the curb resets to zero with a mechanical CHUNK.

Reality is bruising. I can feel it. Not with my senses -- with something deeper. The ZPE field itself is destabilizing. Two identical quantum signatures in close proximity, interfering with each other the way two identical sound waves create destructive interference. Except instead of silence, we're getting reality fractures.

I run.

Away from him. No direction except away. My feet hit pavement and the headache screams with every impact but I do not stop. Behind me, I hear another window crack. A transformer on a telephone pole buzzes and pops. Someone screams -- not at me, at the failing streetlight that's now throwing sparks.

I run for six blocks. Then I walk. Then I lean against a building and vomit into a gutter.

My vision is still doubled. One perspective, at least -- the third is gone, collapsed back into wherever it came from. But my own sight is bifurcated, the world split along a line I can't focus past.

I make it back to the Goodwill. Back to the pod. Schrodinger takes one look at me and flattens against the far wall.

I activate the jump.

The headache lasts six hours in the next dimension. The nosebleed takes forty minutes to stop. My hands shake for the rest of the day.

And the double vision lingers. Not constantly -- in flashes. Like afterimages. For two days, I catch glimpses of a sidewalk that isn't there, a coffee cup in a hand that isn't mine.

The dimension retains a bruise. I know this the way I know the ZPE field is structured -- not from instruments, but from the data I've been accumulating for eighty-two dimensions. Reality there is damaged. Locally, around that street corner, the quantum fabric is thin. Unstable. It will heal -- probably -- but the scar will remain. A weak point in that dimension's structure. Because I walked down the wrong street at the wrong time and came face-to-face with myself.

New rule. I write it in letters twice the size of any other rule on the wall.

15. NEVER MEET YOURSELF.

I circle it. Then circle it again.

Underneath, smaller:

The headache is a warning. The nosebleed is a consequence. The cracking glass is the universe telling you to leave.

Listen to the universe.


                                *


It takes me a week to feel normal again.

I park in dimension eighty-three -- a quiet, uninhabited world with breathable air and a strong ZPE field. Forest. Clean water. No people. Perfect for recovery.

I run the medical scanner. Cellular age: 43.2 years. Unchanged by the encounter. The non-aging is consistent even through near-catastrophic quantum self-interference.

Cortisol levels: through the roof. Heart rate variability: terrible. Blood pressure: elevated.

The scanner can measure the physical toll. What it can't measure is the memory of looking into my own eyes from thirty yards away and feeling reality tear at the seams.

I've been afraid before. The thin-air dimension. The empty town. The jail cell. Fear is familiar now, the way a commuter is familiar with traffic.

This was different. This was the fear of something wrong at a level beneath fear. A wrongness in the structure of existence itself. The feeling that I was looking at a paradox made flesh, and the paradox was looking back.

"Okay," I tell Schrodinger. He's sitting in my lap. He hasn't left my lap in three days, which is his version of intensive care. "New policy. The map shows harmonic signatures. If a dimension's harmonics are too close to home baseline, I look for population centers from a distance first. If the population is human, I assume there's a version of me in that city. I stay out of cities in near-home dimensions. Period."

He purrs.

"Glad you agree."

16. In near-home dimensions, avoid population centers.

17. If you feel a headache starting for no reason, RUN.

Dimension ninety-one.

The map has been steering me toward a cluster of dimensions with strong research infrastructure. Advanced Earths. Places where physics has progressed further, or differently, or both. I've been collecting data, filling gaps in the map, trying to understand the larger structure.

I find this dimension's version of the Internet. It takes about twenty minutes to learn the interface -- different operating system, different browser, same basic concept. Networked information. Search functionality. Publicly accessible databases.

I search for zero-point energy.

Results pour in. Papers. Articles. Conference proceedings. An entire field, robust and active, with hundreds of researchers across dozens of universities.

I search for the foundational papers. The early work that got the field started.

And there it is.

"Zero-Point Field Structure and Dimensional Resonance: A Theoretical Framework."

My paper. My work. The theoretical framework I spent six years developing. The math I scrawled on whiteboards and talked through with Schrodinger at three in the morning and finally published in a journal that two hundred people read.

Published here under the name Dr. Elias Thorne.

I read the paper. All thirty-seven pages. It's my work. Not inspired by my work. Not building on my work. MY work. The same equations. The same derivations. The same leaps of logic in the same order. The notation is slightly different -- cosmetic changes, a few substituted variables -- but the substance is identical.

I search for Henry Taylor in this dimension's academic records.

Nothing.

No Henry Taylor at any university. No Henry Taylor in any physics department. No Henry Taylor in any publication, any conference, any grant proposal.

I don't exist here. But my work does. Under Thorne's name.

I sit at the terminal for a long time.

Schrodinger is on my shoulder. He can feel the tension in my body -- I know because the neural interface from the telepathic dimension gives me a faint impression of his emotional state when we're in contact, and right now his state is concerned. Not anxious. Cats don't really do anxious. But alert. Watchful. The way he gets when the weather changes.

"He stole it," I say quietly. "Or -- not stole it. He IS it. He had the same ideas because he IS the same thing. Thorne is Thorne is Thorne. Every dimension. Same man. Same work."

But that's not right either. Because in my home dimension, Thorne didn't publish the ZPE framework. I did. Thorne funded the research. He built the Institute. He provided the resources. But the theoretical work was mine.

Unless it wasn't.

Unless Thorne knew the theory before I derived it. Unless he funded me not to DISCOVER the framework but to RECREATE it. To independently derive something he already knew.

Why? Why would he do that?

I don't have an answer. I have a pattern. Thorne appears across dimensions. Always connected to ZPE research. Sometimes as a funder. Sometimes as a researcher. Sometimes as something behind walls I can't breach.

And now: sometimes as the author of my work.

I log it. I save copies of every paper. I download Thorne's publication history, his biographical information, his institutional affiliations.

The pod wall gets another entry, but this one isn't a survival rule. It's a question:

Thorne -- same man across dimensions? How?

I don't circle it. I underline it three times.

16. In near-home dimensions, avoid population centers.
17. If you feel a headache for no reason, RUN.

Plus the note about Thorne. Plus hash marks counting dimensions. Plus a small drawing Schrodinger contributed by stepping on the Sharpie and walking across the wall, which I've decided to interpret as abstract expressionism.

The pod's interior is a survival manual, a field journal, and a conspiracy board.

It's also home. The only home I have.

I look at the rules. Seventeen entries from ninety-four dimensions. Each one a scar. Each one a lesson learned at a price I wish I hadn't paid.

And somewhere underneath the rules, in the handwriting of a man who was scared and oxygen-deprived and alone in a phone booth at the edge of reality, the first two entries:

Hold breath. Test breath. Assess.

Stay near the pod.

I was a physicist.

I am a scout. A scavenger. A fugitive from a county jail in a dimension I'll never see again. A man who knows what it feels like to be dismissed for his gender, to stand mute in a city of telepaths, to look into his own eyes and feel the universe crack.

I am a man with a cat and a map and a wall full of rules written in Sharpie.

And I am starting to understand that the rules aren't just about survival.

They're about becoming someone who can survive.

The next dimension is waiting. Schrodinger is watching the HUD with his one good eye, tail wrapped around his paws, ready.

I activate the jump.
