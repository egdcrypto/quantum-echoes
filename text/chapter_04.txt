Chapter 4

WHISPERS ACROSS DIMENSIONS


I've been here three days.

I think. The HUD says seventy-one hours, but the HUD is calibrated to a reality that doesn't apply anymore, so take that with a grain of salt the size of a small planet.

The light doesn't change here. The walls are covered in bioluminescent fungi that pulse at some rhythm I can't figure out. Could be hours. Could be minutes. I tried timing it. I gave up. Too many variables. No control group. Bad science all around.

Okay. What do I know?

The air is breathable. Heavy, metallic, wrong in a way I can taste but can't name. But my lungs work, my blood oxygenates, and I'm not dead.

That's the whole list of things I know.

What I don't know: everything else.


                                *


The beings. I've been calling them the Watchers, because they watch me constantly. Those huge dark eyes tracking my every move with an intensity that's either scientific curiosity or the way you look at something you're considering eating.

They keep their distance. They observe. They whisper among themselves in this clicking, whistling language. Occasionally one approaches and places a small object near me -- a smooth stone, a piece of something that might be food, a crystal that glows faintly when I touch it.

Then retreats.

Gifts? Communication attempts? Appetizers?

No way to tell.

Schrodinger has no such concerns. He walked up to the Watchers on day one, allowed himself to be examined, accepted what I'm fairly sure was an offering of raw fish, and has since established himself as the most socially competent member of our two-person expedition.

He's currently curled up between two of them.

Purring.

The cat is better at first contact than I am. I'm going to put that in whatever journal survives this.


                                *


The pod sits where I arrived. Dull silver. Phone-booth-sized. It looks like someone left a refrigerator in the middle of an alien cave.

I've been running diagnostics. Testing systems. Trying to figure out what happened.

Here's the situation. The State Bridge in Elias's lab was a massive installation -- coils, matrices, generators filling an entire room. The pod is the portable version I'd been building in parallel. Condensed. Simplified. Designed for samples. Test objects. Certainly not for a grown man and a cat.

But when the State Bridge surged and the field caught me, the pod came along for the ride.

Why? Three theories:

One: it was entangled with the larger machine.
Two: the ZPE field wrapped around the nearest compatible technology and dragged it.
Three: the universe has a sense of humor.

I'm going with all three.

The important thing is: I have it. A small, portable Quantum State Bridge, powered by zero-point energy. And ZPE exists everywhere. Even here. Even in this alien dimension. The HUD confirms it -- the field readings are different from home, but the field is present. The pod can draw power.

Which means, in theory, I can jump again.

In theory.

In practice: the pod has no targeting system. The original State Bridge didn't either. We hadn't gotten that far. We were still trying to superpose a tungsten ball. Navigation was about fourteen steps ahead on the research timeline.

So if I activate the pod, I'll go somewhere. Just don't know where. Or when. Or whether the air will be breathable.

That last one keeps me up at night.

Assuming it's night.


                                *


Day five, according to the HUD.

I've made the decision.

It's not a brave decision. It's a digestive one. The food the Watchers have been offering is sustaining me, but I have no idea what I'm eating, and my GI tract is sending increasingly urgent memos about the situation. The water tastes like copper. The metallic tang in the air is getting stronger, or I'm getting more sensitive to it.

Either way: time to go.

I need to find a reality with pizza. Or at least recognizable carbohydrates.

Collecting Schrodinger takes twenty minutes because he's embedded himself in the Watcher social hierarchy and does not appreciate being extracted. One of the taller Watchers makes a sound -- low, resonant, like a cello string being plucked.

I find myself apologizing to an alien for repossessing my own cat.

"Sorry," I say, Schrodinger squirming under my arm. "He's mine. Technically. He doesn't always agree."

The Watcher blinks. Unreadable.

I step into the pod. Schrodinger settles against my chest, purring against my sternum. I pull up the HUD, configure the activation sequence -- same basic process as the State Bridge, scaled down, simplified. Energy readings: stable. ZPE field: strong.

Destination: no idea.

"Okay," I say to the cat. "Cosmic dice roll. You ready?"

He blinks.

I activate the pod.


                                *


I take a breath.

Something is wrong.

My lungs expand. Air enters. But it's like drinking water through a coffee stirrer. My body is going through the motions of breathing, but the return on investment is terrible. Heart rate spikes immediately. Brain screaming for oxygen.

Okay. Assessment.

Rolling hills. Sky is the wrong shade of blue -- almost violet. Clouds too thin, too high. Everything washed out, pale.

The air is thin. Mountain thin. I'm guessing fourteen percent oxygen instead of the twenty-one my body was designed for.

Okay. Math time.

At fourteen percent O2, I've got maybe ten minutes before my judgment starts to deteriorate. Twenty before I'm making decisions that could kill me. An hour before the decisions are made for me.

Schrodinger, the absolute bastard, seems completely fine. He's sitting on top of the pod, surveying the violet landscape like a conqueror. Cats apparently handle low oxygen better than humans.

Or this cat is simply too stubborn to acknowledge physical distress.

I don't admire the scenery. I don't scan the horizon.

I get back in the pod.

My fingers are clumsy on the HUD. Hypoxia already -- and I've been here less than two minutes. That's terrifying. I initiate the activation sequence. The pod hums. The ZPE field engages.

Just before the jump, one thought crystallizes:

The universe doesn't care if I can breathe.

Not hostile. Not malicious. Just indifferent. The multiverse is an ocean, and I'm a fish that jumped out of one bowl and landed in another, and the water might be salt instead of fresh, and nobody's checking.

The jump takes me.


                                *


I gasp in the new dimension's air like a drowning man breaking the surface.

Breathable. Barely. Too much CO2 -- I can feel the slight euphoria, the giddiness that means my blood chemistry is shifting. But I can function. I can think.

Which means I can make a plan.

I pull a Sharpie from my pocket. Black. The kind I used to label equipment in the lab. Somehow still clipped to my shirt through an interdimensional transfer.

Side note: this Sharpie may be the most resilient piece of engineering in this entire endeavor. Nobel Prize for material science. I'm nominating it.

I write on the inside of the pod's housing:

1. Hold breath. Test breath. Assess.
2. Stay near the pod.

Two rules. Block letters. Written on the interior of a quantum transportation device.

It looks like safety instructions written by a man who has accepted that safety is now a relative concept.

It's the most important thing I've ever written.


                                *


Dimension four.

Earth-like. Breathable. I take my test breath and the air is clean, temperate, almost pleasant. Small town. 1940s architecture. Neat houses, trimmed lawns, brick storefronts. Water tower. Church steeple. A flag snapping in the wind.

No people.

Not "no people visible." No people. The town is maintained, clean, cared for -- and completely empty. No footsteps. No voices. No cars running. Just the wind and the flag and a clock ticking in a storefront window for nobody.

Schrodinger will not leave the pod.

This has never happened. The cat has explored alien caves. Endured thin air. Walked up to beings that towered over him by five feet. He has never once refused to leave the pod.

He's pressed against the back wall. One eye wide. Ears flat. Every hair standing up.

He is communicating, with the full eloquence available to a one-eyed tabby, that this place is wrong.

I should listen to the cat.

I don't listen to the cat.

I explore the town for two days. I find food in the stores -- canned goods, still sealed, still edible. Clothing. A backpack in a sporting goods store, sturdy canvas, big enough to carry supplies. First aid kit from a pharmacy. Flashlight. Batteries. A knife from a hardware store.

I take everything I can carry.

Because here's the lesson: the multiverse is not a tourist destination. It's a wilderness. You don't walk into a wilderness without supplies.

Day two: I pack the pod. Tape the first aid kit to the interior wall. Stash canned food in the footwell. Hang the backpack from a hook I fashion out of bent wire.

The pod goes from empty phone booth to survival closet.

Cramped. Cluttered. Marginally less likely to kill me.

Schrodinger watches me pack from inside the pod, where he has remained for the entire two days. He will not set foot outside.

And the longer I stay, the more I get it.

The town isn't just empty. It's wrong. Not the absence of sound -- the quality of it. A town that's quiet because people are sleeping sounds different from a town that's quiet because people are gone. This town is the second kind.

And the longer I stand in it, the more I think whatever emptied it might still be here.

New rule. I add it to the pod wall:

3. If the cat won't leave, you don't leave.

I jump. I don't look back.

But I'm wearing a new backpack. And the pod is stocked.


                                *


After the empty town, I change.

Every safe dimension becomes a supply run. I'm still cataloguing, still observing, still trying to understand the multiverse. But now I'm doing it while filling a water bottle, pocketing batteries, testing whether the fruit in that forest is edible.

My scientific method for alien produce: eat it, wait an hour. Not dead yet? Good enough. Into the bag.

Not rigorous. But the multiverse strips away academic rigor pretty fast. You adapt or you die, and I'm not ready to die.

Dimension by dimension, the inventory grows. Water purification bottle from an Earth-like outdoor supply store. Compact sleeping bag from another. A crude atmospheric tester I build from salvaged electronics -- a sensor wired to a display, measuring oxygen and CO2.

It's slow. It's unreliable. It's considerably better than holding my breath and hoping.

Rule four:

4. Scavenge every safe dimension. You don't know when the next one is.

I learn to read dimensions the way a sailor reads weather. Arriving in a new reality becomes a sequence: hold breath, test breath, check the sensor, watch the cat.

Schrodinger's behavior is more reliable than any instrument I've built.

Ears flat: danger.
Refuses to exit: leave immediately.
Wanders freely: air is good.
Sticks close: marginal. Breathable but wrong in some way he can sense and I can't.
Purring: safe enough to sleep.

Rules five and six:

5. Three days minimum in any breathable dimension. Rest. Eat. Think.
6. Trust the cat.

That last one isn't a joke.


                                *


I keep records.

The HUD's data storage is crude, but it works. I log each dimension: atmospheric composition, gravity, temperature, light, habitability, and a general assessment I've started calling the Schrodinger Rating.

Because the cat's opinion has become the most reliable metric I have.

Some entries:

Dimension 1: Alien cave. Breathable. Bioluminescent. Residents: tall, blue, curious. Departed voluntarily. Schrodinger Rating: 8/10 (made friends).

Dimension 2: Violet sky. Thin air. ~14% O2. Uninhabitable. Duration: 90 seconds. Schrodinger Rating: 6/10 (enjoyed the view, untroubled by hypoxia, because of course he was).

Dimension 3: High CO2. Euphoric. Mildly toxic. Overgrown vegetation. No visible fauna. Duration: 4 hours. Schrodinger Rating: 9/10 (chased something in the undergrowth; unknown if he caught it; refused to discuss).

Dimension 4: Earth-like. Breathable. Small town, 1940s. No inhabitants. Eerily quiet. Supplies acquired. Duration: 2 days. Schrodinger Rating: 3/10 (refused to leave pod; unprecedented; trust the cat).


                                *


Dimension twelve.

The loneliness sets in.

Not gradually. Like a switch flipping. One morning -- if there are mornings in the multiverse; I've decided to measure time by when I sleep, which is the only clock I have left -- I wake up in the pod with Schrodinger pressed against my side. And the weight of it just hits me.

I'm alone.

Not just far from home. Far from the concept of home. There's no home to go back to. I don't know how to get there. The pod doesn't have a "return" button. Every jump is a roll of dice in a game whose rules I'm learning by dying slightly less each time.

I miss Lawson.

Not the way you miss someone in another city. That kind of missing has a direction. You can point at it on a map. This missing has no direction. Lawson is in a reality I can't reach, and the distance isn't measured in miles but in the fundamental structure of the universe.

She's nineteen. She's alone. Her mother is dead, and her father vanished, and the only explanation she has is a note that says it's Schrodinger.

She'll figure it out.

She has to figure it out.

Because if she doesn't, then I'm not a father who left a door. I'm just a father who left.

I hold the cat tighter. He tolerates it. Three seconds longer than usual.

That's his version of a hug.


                                *


Dimension seventeen.

I materialize in a forest. Tall trees, broad leaves, dappled light filtering through a high canopy.

Test breath.

Oh.

The air is perfect. Clean, rich, the kind of air that makes your lungs remember what they were designed for.

I pull out the atmospheric sensor. Twenty-one percent oxygen. Textbook Earth levels.

I just stand there and breathe.

Schrodinger leaps out of the pod and vanishes into the ferns. Maximum confidence. Schrodinger Rating: off the charts. The cat's verdict is in.

I should scan the area. Check the HUD. Assess for threats.

Instead, I sit down on the forest floor, my back against a tree, and I cry.

Not the dignified kind. The ugly kind. Snot and shaking and sounds that don't belong to a forty-three-year-old physicist. The kind that comes from somewhere so deep you didn't know it was there until the ground opened up.

I cry for Lawson. For the look on her face when she finds the empty lab. For the weekends I was absent, the years I spent inside equations instead of inside her life.

I cry for Gayelyn. Seven years dead and I still reach for her side of the bed in the morning.

I cry because the air is good and I have a backpack full of scavenged supplies and six rules written in Sharpie on the wall of a quantum machine, and none of it matters because the people I love are in a reality I can't reach.

Schrodinger comes back. He's carrying something in his mouth -- a small, iridescent insect, still alive, legs waving.

He drops it at my feet.

"Thanks, buddy," I say, wiping my face.

He sits down next to me. Not on me. Next to me. Close enough that I can feel his warmth. Doesn't purr. Just sits.

We stay in dimension seventeen for five days. I sleep under the trees. I drink from a stream after running it through the purification bottle. I eat things that are probably fruit (tested: not dead after an hour) and supplement with canned goods from the empty town. I refill the water supply. Repair a tear in the backpack.

I talk to Schrodinger about quantum mechanics, about Lawson, about the Cubs' chances this season, about the statistical probability of finding a dimension with a working pizza oven.

He listens with the patience of someone who has heard it all before and expects to hear it all again.

Day five. I feel ready to jump. Not healed -- you don't heal from what I'm carrying. But functional. Rested. Resupplied.

I log it:

Dimension 17: Forest. Perfect air. Safe. Beautiful. Return candidate. Schrodinger Rating: 10/10.

Personal note: Cried here. Needed to.


                                *


Dimension twenty-five.

I've noticed a pattern.

The truly alien dimensions don't hurt. The caves, the thin-air worlds, the places where physics is visibly different -- I can compartmentalize those. They aren't home. They aren't trying to be.

It's the almost-homes that destroy me.

A city with cars and buildings and people on sidewalks, except the signage is in a language I don't recognize. A suburban neighborhood where the houses are the right shape but the sky is faintly green. A university campus that looks almost exactly like mine, except the buildings are rearranged and the trees are a species I've never seen.

Every familiar detail -- a stop sign, a park bench, the smell of coffee from a shop window -- is followed by a detail that's wrong.

Close but no. Not home. Never home.

I've learned to scavenge the almost-homes fast. Get in, take what I need, don't linger. Don't walk past the park where a man is pushing a child on a swing. Don't stop at the coffee shop. Don't look at the families.

Schrodinger doesn't like the almost-homes either. Gets restless. Tail flicking. Ears rotating. I think he can feel it too -- the nearness of a place where he used to sleep on a stack of journals in a lab that smelled like pipe tobacco.

Or maybe he just doesn't like green skies. Hard to tell with cats.


                                *


Dimension thirty.

The pod's interior looks like a survivalist's closet.

Inventory:

- Backpack from the empty town, on its hook
- Waterproof duffel from a marina in an almost-home
- First aid kit taped to the wall, supplemented from three dimensions
- Water purification bottle
- Backup water container
- Four cans of food in a mesh sling
- Sleeping bag rolled tight against the ceiling
- Flashlight with spare batteries in a Ziploc bag
- Knife on a carabiner clipped to the door handle
- Coil of rope (not sure I'll ever use it; feel better having it)

I've also built atmospheric sensor version two. Assembled from electronics scavenged across five dimensions. More sensitive oxygen reader. CO2 detector. Crude spectrometer that can identify a handful of common toxins.

It's held together with electrical tape and optimism.

It gives me maybe sixty seconds of warning before my lungs would tell me the same thing. But sixty seconds is the difference between walking back to the pod and crawling.

The Sharpie rules have grown. The first two were scrawled in a shaking hand, oxygen-deprived and terrified. By rule six, my penmanship has improved. So has my survival rate.

Go-bag stays packed at all times. Backpack, water, food, first aid, knife, flashlight. If I have to jump on zero notice -- bad air, hostile environment, the cat freaking out -- I grab the bag and go.

I was a physicist. I am becoming a scout.

There's no training montage. It's a hundred small adjustments, each one born from a mistake that almost killed me. The go-bag exists because I jumped into a toxic atmosphere and wasted thirty seconds gathering loose supplies. The backup water exists because I went two days without finding a drinkable source. The rope exists because I fell down a ravine in a heavy-gravity dimension and spent four hours climbing out with bleeding hands.

Every piece of gear is a scar.


                                *


Dimension thirty-four. Earth-like. Breathable.

A city I don't recognize.

I'm being careful. Pod hidden behind a dumpster in an alley. Not elegant, but the multiverse has stripped me of remaining dignity. I venture out with the go-bag to gather intel: where am I, when am I, is this a reality I can spend time in?

Newsstand. Papers in English. The date -- I check three times -- is four years before I left my home dimension.

I'm in the past. Not my past, but a past.

And on the front page, under a headline about a new research facility in Central Texas, I see a name.

Thorne.

Dr. Elias Thorne. Pictured at a groundbreaking ceremony. Same face. Same eccentric, too-intense expression. Same eyes that look like they're seeing seven things at once.

Different dimension. Same Thorne.

I stare at the photograph. Schrodinger, perched on my shoulder because I've stopped caring about looking normal in public, stares at it too.

"He's here," I say.

Not "here" as in this city. "Here" as in this reality.

It's the first time I've encountered a familiar name across dimensions. It won't be the last. By dimension fifty, I'll have seen Thorne's name, or his face, or his Institute, or the shadow of his influence, in over a dozen different realities. Different scales. Different methods. Sometimes a university. Sometimes a corporation. Sometimes something behind walls I can't breach.

But always Thorne.

Always the same man.

I don't know what it means yet. I file it the way I file the odd resonance patterns in my blood tests -- as data I don't have a framework for.

Interesting. Concerning. Incomplete.

Lawson, if she were here, would already be building the framework. She'd have a theory by dimension twelve and a working model by dimension twenty. She's faster than me. Always has been.

But she's not here. And I'm alone with a cat and a pod and a go-bag and a growing certainty that the man who funded my research, who built my lab, who smiled at me over scotch and said "I can tune into it" -- that man is woven into the fabric of reality in a way I don't understand.

Coincidence? Or pattern?

I don't have enough data.

I hate not having enough data.


                                *


I jump again.

And again. And again.

The pod hums. The cat judges. The dice roll. The go-bag rides on my back like muscle memory, the way you check your pockets for your keys before leaving the house.

Some dimensions are breathable. Some aren't. Some have people. Some don't. Some look like home and some look like nothing I've ever imagined.

I hold my breath before every jump. Check the sensor. Watch the cat. Stay near the pod.

I used to explain superposition to my daughter with a spinning coin. Now I scavenge canned goods and build atmospheric sensors from salvaged electronics and write survival rules in Sharpie on the inside of a quantum machine. The lab coat is gone. The theories are footnotes.

What's left is simpler: breathe, eat, move, survive. Find the next dimension. Stay alive.

Looking for what?

I don't know. I tell myself I'm looking for home. But home is a direction I can't point toward, a daughter I can't reach.

What I'm really looking for is a reason to keep jumping.

Meanwhile, Schrodinger sits a little straighter after every departure. His one eye fixed on some middle distance, tail wrapped tight around his paws. He made friends in that alien cave. And then I put him back in a box and took him somewhere else.

A cat named Schrodinger. Living in a box. Traveling between realities. Never knowing what he'll find when the lid opens.

Both alive and dead.

Mostly alive, though.

Mostly.
