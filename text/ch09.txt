Chapter 7

THE IMPOSSIBLE REUNION


She doesn't run.

I want to put that in context. A woman alone on a walking trail. A man steps out who looks exactly like her dead husband. The normal responses are: scream, pepper spray, call 911, all of the above.

She doesn't do any of those things.

She puts her hand on a live oak and goes white. Like, hospital white. Like someone just drained all the blood out of her face and replaced it with skim milk.

"Henry?"

And I think: okay. This is it. Say the speech. You rehearsed the speech. Three weeks in a cabin, talking to a cat, practicing this exact moment. A careful, methodical, rational explanation that eases her into the impossible without breaking anything.

I open my mouth.

Nothing comes out.

The speech is gone. Every word of it. Instead my brain is playing a highlight reel of Gayelyn humming in the kitchen. Off-key. Always off-key. She never knew she was doing it. And the sound is so clear in my head that I can actually hear it, and I'm standing on a trail with tears on my face like an idiot who forgot how to talk.

Two hundred and fourteen dimensions and I can't say hello.

I should back up.

Schrodinger saw her first. The cat. We came around the bend by the live oaks and he just stopped. Sat down in the middle of the path. Full predator lock. That absolute cat stillness where they turn into a furry security camera.

I followed his gaze. Thirty yards ahead. Gayelyn.

My legs almost buckled.

The cat is between us now. Purring. Equidistant. Like a furry mediator at the world's weirdest custody hearing.


*


"You're not--" She starts. Stops. Starts again. Her voice is shaking. Her hands are shaking. "You can't be. He's dead. I was at the funeral. I--"

"I know." My voice sounds like I gargled gravel. "I know he's dead. I'm not him."

"Then who--"

"I'm Henry Taylor. But I'm not your Henry Taylor." Deep breath. "I'm from somewhere else. A different version. Of the world."

She stares at me.

"That's insane."

"Yes."

"You look..." Hard swallow. "You look exactly like him. Not similar. Exactly."

"I know."

"How is that possible?"

"Long answer. You're going to think I'm crazy for about ninety percent of it. But I can prove it."

She takes a step back. Not running. Thinking. Gayelyn always steps back from things she needs to understand. My Gayelyn did it too. It's like she's trying to get a wider angle on reality.

"Prove it how?"

"By telling you things only Henry would know."


*


We sit on a bench.

This was not the plan. The plan involved a carefully chosen location, a calm setting, a gradual introduction. The plan did not involve a random park bench twenty feet from a playground where a woman is pushing a toddler on a swing.

Normal life. Right there. While I explain parallel universes to a widow.

I start small. I'm rushing it. I know I'm rushing it. I can't stop.

"You hum when you cook. Always off-key. You don't know you're doing it." The words are coming out too fast. Slow down. "Your mother did it too. You told me that -- after Thanksgiving, we were doing dishes, and you said you didn't realize you'd picked it up until you heard yourself humming and thought for a second your mom was in the kitchen."

Her hand goes to her mouth.

"You sleep on the left side of the bed but by morning you're in the middle. You steal the covers. You don't believe you steal the covers." I'm talking faster now. I can't help it. "I used to take photos at 3 a.m. -- you, wrapped up like a human burrito -- and show them to you at breakfast. You said I staged them."

A sound comes out of her. Laugh and sob at the same time.

"Your favorite movie is The Princess Bride. You can quote the entire sword fight. You put hot sauce on scrambled eggs but literally nothing else, which -- you admitted this yourself -- makes zero logical sense. You cry at dog commercials. Every. Single. Time."

"Stop." A whisper.

She doesn't mean stop.

"The night you told me you were pregnant." My voice cracks. Just breaks in half. "You didn't tell me. You left the test on my desk. On top of Temmer's Quantum Mechanics textbook. And when I found it you were standing in the doorway and you said--"

"'Schrodinger's baby.'" Her voice is almost gone. "'Both pregnant and not pregnant until you look at the test.'"

The swing creaks. The toddler laughs. A dog barks somewhere.

"How." Gayelyn's voice is flat now. Not asking. Demanding. "How do you know that. Nobody knows that. I never told anyone. That was ours. Just me and Henry."

"Because I am Henry. A different Henry. From a different version of this world. And in my version I said the same thing to you, in the same doorway, and you laughed the same way, and nine months later we named our daughter Lawson."

Her eyes go wide.

"Daughter?"

"Daughter. Lawson Marie Taylor. She has your eyes and my stubbornness and she's the smartest person I've ever met."

Gayelyn is crying. Not the photogenic kind. The real kind. The kind where your face does things you'd be embarrassed about later.

"We had a son. Lawson."

"I know. I saw him. At the memorial."

Sharp look. "You were at the memorial?"

"Back row. Hat and sunglasses. Didn't stay long." Pause. "He looks just like her, Gayelyn. Same eyes. Same head-tilt thing when he's thinking."

She stares at me for a long time. Still crying. But underneath the crying, something is shifting. She's not deciding whether to believe me. The details are too specific for a stranger to fake.

She's deciding what to do about it.


*


I tell her about the hummingbird feeder.

This is my ace. The thing no amount of research could turn up, because it existed in a space so private that even close friends wouldn't know.

"Second anniversary. I built you a hummingbird feeder." I'm trying to slow down. Failing. "Not store-bought. I made it in the garage. Cedar and driftwood from that coast trip. I carved a little perch on the side because -- you said you loved how they hovered but you wanted to see one be still. Just for a second. You wanted to know what a hummingbird looked like when it stopped moving."

She grabs the bench. White-knuckled.

"We hung it outside the kitchen window and every morning you'd stand at the sink and watch them come. You said they reminded you of your mother. Always moving, always busy. And I said they reminded me of you." I'm losing the thread here. The words are getting jumbled. "And you said 'I'm nothing like a hummingbird' and I said -- I said you were exactly like a hummingbird because you didn't know how to be still."

She's shaking her head. Not no. Just... wonder.

"He made me one too." A whisper. "My Henry. Driftwood perch. Kitchen window. Said the same thing about my mother."

Of course he did.

Same man. Different dimension. Same driftwood. Same cedar. Same impulse to show the woman he loved what a hummingbird looked like when it finally stopped.


*


"Explain it to me." Her voice has steadied. The tears are still there but underneath them is the woman I married. Steel and warmth. "How is this possible."

Okay. Park bench. October. One-eyed cat. Playground. Here goes.

"Think of it like pages in a book," I say. "Each page is a different reality. A different version of the world. They're side by side but separate. You can't see the next page from the one you're on."

She's listening. Not the polite kind. The real kind.

"Now, that metaphor is actually grounded in physics. There's something called the Casimir effect -- in 1948, a physicist predicted that if you put two metal plates really close together, nanometers apart, the empty space between them behaves differently than the empty space outside. Different wavelengths of energy fit in the gap. The imbalance creates a force. A real, measurable force that pushes the plates together."

"Nothing pushes them together?"

"Exactly. Two plates. Nothing between them. And they push together. They proved it experimentally in 1997. The empty space between those plates is like the space between pages in the book. It's not really empty. It's seething with energy -- what physicists call vacuum fluctuations. Particles popping in and out of existence faster than you can measure."

I'm watching her face. She's tracking. Gayelyn always tracked faster than she let on.

"So the space between the pages -- between realities -- isn't nothing. It's a field. Zero-point energy. The lowest possible energy state of the quantum vacuum. And my colleague Elias Thorne figured out that this field doesn't just sit there. It connects things. Different realities exist as different frequencies within this field, the way different radio stations exist on the same electromagnetic spectrum. Your reality is one frequency. Mine is another. They're playing simultaneously, occupying the same space, but you can only hear one at a time."

"And you built a radio."

I stare at her.

"The machine. The State Bridge. It's a tuner."

Two hundred and fourteen dimensions. Dozens of attempts to explain this to myself in a notebook. And Gayelyn gets it in four sentences.

"Yes. That's exactly what it is. A tuner. It shifts you from one frequency to another. Same space, different signal. And when the accident happened -- when the machine fired wrong -- it shifted me and the cat out of our home frequency and we've been bouncing through the dial ever since."

"And you can't tune back to the original station."

"Not yet. The original machine is in my home dimension. I've got a portable version -- the pod -- but it doesn't have fine control. Every jump, I'm scanning for the next strong signal and hoping."

Long silence. The swing creaks. Schrodinger has treed a squirrel and is sitting at the base of the oak looking personally offended that squirrels can climb.

Gayelyn looks at me. Really looks. The way you look at something you're afraid will vanish.

"You know what this sounds like."

"Insane."

"No. Well, yes." Pause. She tucks a strand of hair behind her ear.

I stop breathing.

She doesn't know she does that. She never knew. It's automatic. Hand up, find the strand, tuck it behind the ear. My Gayelyn did it too. Same gesture. Same unconscious habit. Two hundred and fourteen dimensions and this woman still tucks her hair behind her ear like that.

"It sounds like something Henry used to talk about." She has no idea she just wrecked me. "About God."

"God?"

"Quantum entanglement. He used to say that theologians borrowed the concept. Two particles connected instantly, no matter how far apart. He said it was the closest physics had come to explaining omnipresence."

Something jolts through me. Not a thought. A physical shock, like touching a live wire.

I said the same thing. Almost word for word. To Lawson. When she was fourteen. The same phrase, the same framing, in another dimension. Two Henry Taylors, independently arriving at the same sentence. That's not coincidence. That's not even parallel evolution. That's the field. The hum. The thing that's been constant across every dimension I've visited, delivering the same intuition to the same man in different realities.

I don't say any of this. I'm not ready to say it. But the jolt stays, humming in my chest like a tuning fork that won't go quiet.

"He was right. More right than he knew. The connections don't just cross space and time. They cross dimensions." Pause. "What I'm standing in right now is proof. Two realities. Two Henry Taylors. Connected by the same memories. The same love. The same..." I'm reaching for a word and the only one that fits is one that doesn't belong in a physics conversation. "Soul, maybe. If you'll let me use that word."

"I'll allow it." And there it is. The ghost of a smile. Small, fragile, the first one. "Henry would have allowed it too. He always said the best physics happens at the edge of what you can prove."


*


She doesn't bring me home that day.

Fair enough.

We meet on the trail three more times over the next week. Each meeting is longer. She asks questions -- Gayelyn questions, precise and incisive, the kind that make you realize you haven't actually thought through your own position. I answer as honestly as I can, which means sometimes the answer is "I don't know" and sometimes the answer is uncomfortable silence.

On the fourth meeting, she brings Lawson.

A call on my burner phone first. "He wants to meet you. I told him everything. He thinks I've lost my mind but he wants to see for himself."

So I'm sitting on the bench. Same bench. Schrodinger beside me, grooming his paw with the profound indifference of a being who has zero investment in human emotional milestones.

They come around the bend. Mother and son.

And I see him.

The shape of his face. The walk -- slightly pigeon-toed, the same way my Lawson walks, a gait I'd recognize across any distance in any dimension. The jaw. The cautious eyes. He's Lawson, refracted through a different lens.

He stops ten feet away. Gayelyn keeps walking. He doesn't. Plants his feet. Crosses his arms. Studies me.

I know that look. I've seen it in the mirror. A Taylor evaluating data before committing to a conclusion.

"So. You're my dad from another dimension."

No buildup. Straight to it. Lawson does the same thing.

"Yes."

"Prove it."

I glance at Gayelyn. She gives me a tiny shrug. You're on your own.

"When you were seven," I say, picking a memory I hope is universal, "you asked your dad why the sky was blue. And he didn't just tell you about Rayleigh scattering. He took you outside with a garden hose and a flashlight. He made a mist and shone the light through it so you could actually see the scattering. And you said--"

"'Can we make it red?'" Lawson finishes. His arms uncross. Just slightly. "He used a red filter. Mom's reading glasses case."

"An orange one," I say. "In my reality. Gas station sunglasses. Same idea."

Lawson stares at me. I can see the war behind his eyes. Brain versus evidence. Grief versus hope.

"This is insane."

"Yes."

"You're insane."

"Possibly."

"Mom is insane for believing you."

"Your mother is the sanest person I've ever met. In any dimension."

That lands. The wall cracks. Not much. But enough.

Then I get cocky.

"Your first pet was a hamster," I say. I'm smiling already, confident, on a roll. "Named it Einstein. You kept it in a cage on your desk and it got out and your mom found it in her--"

"I never had a hamster."

The smile falls off my face like it was slapped off.

"What?"

"No hamster. I had a goldfish. Named it Bohr." Pause. "It died in like three weeks."

Silence. The wrong kind. Not the meaningful kind from earlier. The kind where you've just proven that the guy claiming to share your dad's soul doesn't know your childhood pet.

Gayelyn is watching me carefully.

"In my reality," I start, "Lawson had a hamster named--"

"Yeah, I get it. Different dimensions, different pets." Arms crossed again. The crack has sealed over. "What else is different? What else do you think you know about me that's wrong?"

I don't have an answer. The honest answer is: I don't know where the sameness ends and the difference starts. I've been spending memories like currency, buying trust, and I just bounced a check.

"I don't know."

Gayelyn touches his arm. He doesn't shrug her off. Doesn't lean in either.

Nobody talks for a while. Playground noises fill the gap. I'm realizing something uncomfortable. The big things -- the humming, The Princess Bride, Schrodinger's baby, the hummingbird feeder -- those held. But the small things, the daily stuff, the pet you had when you were eight -- those drifted. And the drift matters. Because it means I'm not his father. Not exactly. I'm a guy who shares most of the source code but not all of the runtime.

Lawson looks at Schrodinger. The cat has been watching this whole thing with his one good eye.

"What's his name?"

"Schrodinger."

His mouth twitches. A smile, beaten down but not quite fast enough. "You named your cat Schrodinger."

Gayelyn steps in, softly. "Your father did too. The cat in the lab."

Lawson looks at the cat. The cat looks at Lawson. Some kind of transaction happens between them that I'm not part of.

Schrodinger purrs.

Lawson crouches down and scratches behind his ear. The right one. The side with the good eye.

"He likes that spot."

"Yeah. He does."

Something starts. Not trust. Not forgiveness. Nothing that clean. But something.


*


Over the next few weeks, I learn the shape of their lives.

Gayelyn teaches art history at the community college. A subject so far from quantum physics that I almost laugh, until I remember my Gayelyn minored in it too. Same woman. Same refuge.

Lawson is in his first year. Undeclared. He's good at math -- scary good, the genetic kind -- but he won't touch physics. His father was a physicist. His father is dead. The math is obvious.

They live in the house this Henry bought. Modest ranch on a quiet street. Photos on the walls. This Henry parted his hair on the left. Wore glasses I never needed. Had a scar on his chin from some childhood accident I don't share.

But the smile is the same. The way he holds Gayelyn -- one arm around her waist, always her left side, always pulling her a little closer.

I think about my Lawson constantly. In the spaces between conversations with Gayelyn. In the silences while this Lawson decides whether to trust me. At night in the cabin with Schrodinger on my chest. I think about her at nineteen, standing in my empty lab, holding a note. I think about her at twenty. Twenty-five. Thirty. Growing older in a reality I can't reach.

Every day I stay here is a day I'm not looking for a way back to her.

Every day I stay, the cabin feels less like a hideout and more like a home.

That word scares the hell out of me.


*


Gayelyn starts bringing food to the cabin. Soups, casseroles, the kind of food that says "I have feelings I can't articulate so here is a lasagna." Lawson starts tagging along sometimes. Doesn't talk much. Sits on the porch and throws sticks for the golden retriever next door and occasionally asks me something.

"Did you have a dog?"

"No."

"Dad had a dog when he was a kid. Beagle named Watson. He talked about Watson a lot."

"I had a beagle named Watson when I was eight."

Lawson stares at me. Then at the golden retriever. Then back at me.

"This is so weird."

"Yeah. It really is."

Schrodinger sits on the porch railing. The golden retriever has learned not to test him. The squirrels haven't. They'll learn.

The light fades. Trees go dark. Gayelyn touches my arm before she leaves. Just her fingertips on my sleeve. Quick. The contact goes through me like a current.

Sixteen years of missing this.

"Come to the house. For dinner. A real dinner. At a table."

"Gayelyn--"

"I'm not asking you to move in. I'm asking you to eat something that didn't come from a can."

Fair point. The canned soup situation has gotten dire.

I'm going to stay.

I know this the way I know gravity pulls down and light bends around mass and Schrodinger will always find the warmest spot in any room. I know it below the level of decision. In my bones.

I'm going to stay, and it's the most selfish thing I'll ever do, and it's the most human thing I'll ever do, and I don't think those two facts will ever resolve into one thing or the other.

A woman who tucks her hair behind her ear.

A boy who looks like my daughter.

A cat who has no opinion about any of this.

And tomorrow, apparently, a home-cooked meal.
