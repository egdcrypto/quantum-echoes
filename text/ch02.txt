CHAPTER 2 - THE VANISHING


The morning of the day I disappear, I leave my daughter a note in a book.

This is not unusual. I leave notes everywhere. Have for years. In Lawson's backpack, between the pages of her textbooks, tucked into the pocket of her lab coat -- the one with the coffee stain on the left sleeve that she won't wash because it has "character." I put them in her shoes sometimes. She says this is annoying. She has kept every single one.

Today's note is different.

I'm standing in the kitchen. 6:47 a.m. Lawson is asleep upstairs. She has class at ten but she won't get up until nine-fifteen because she is physically incapable of being early for anything that doesn't involve a physics problem. She gets this from her mother, who was late to our first date by forty minutes and to our wedding by twelve.

God, I loved that woman.

Schrodinger is sitting on the kitchen table. He's not supposed to be on the table.

"Off."

He looks at me. He has heard this word before. He is not persuaded.

"Fine. Stay."

He was going to anyway.

I open the book. Temmer's Quantum Mechanics, third edition. Same copy since grad school. Coffee rings, three colors of margin notes, spine cracked at the superposition chapter. I find pages 142 and 143. I tear a piece from the margin of 144 -- carefully, because even right now I can't bring myself to rip a textbook without precision. Some things are sacred.

And then I write something I didn't plan to write.

My hand moves before I think about it. The pen just goes. Nine words, cramped and hurried, like the pen knows something I don't.

Lawson, it's not you, it's not me, it's Schrodinger.

I stare at it.

That's not what I was going to write. I was going to write something normal. Something like the others. Don't forget you're extraordinary. Your old man loves you. The usual.

But my hand wrote this instead. And I don't know why.

I almost cross it out. The words don't make sense. Not as a goodbye, not as a note of affection, not as anything a father should leave for his daughter in a textbook.

But something in me -- below the equations, below the grief, in a place I don't have instruments to measure -- says leave it.

So I do.

I tuck the note between the pages. Close the book. Put it on the shelf in my home office, third row, between Feynman's Lectures and a dog-eared copy of Cosmos that Lawson gave me for Christmas when she was twelve. She'd written in the front cover: Dad -- the universe is weird and so are we. Love, your favorite quantum state.

Best thing anyone's ever written to me. And yes, I'm including a very nice peer review I once got from Stephen Hawking's research group.

I pour a second cup of coffee. I make one for Lawson too -- way too much sugar, not enough cream, the way she likes it -- and leave it on the counter with a Post-it: Your old man loves you. Don't forget to eat something that isn't caffeine.

Then I pick up the cat.

This is a process. Step one: approach casually. Do not make eye contact. Do not telegraph intent. Step two: one hand under the belly, other hand supporting the hind legs. Step three: lift with an expression of complete nonchalance, as if this is something that is simply happening and nobody planned it. Step four: accept the look of betrayal. Step five: proceed to car.

I've been doing this for seven years. I have it down to about four seconds.

I drive to the lab.

The hum is louder today. The low vibration I've heard my whole career -- the one most physicists dismiss as equipment noise, electrical interference, tinnitus from too many late nights. I've always heard it. In the lab, in the car, in the silence after Gayelyn died. But today it's sharper. Clearer. Like someone turned up the gain on a frequency I'm not supposed to notice.

I attribute it to nerves. My hands are steady but the rest of me is vibrating at a frequency I can't name.

It's not nerves.


*


The Thorne Institute for Advanced Physics sits on a hundred acres of cleared Texas scrubland. Glass and steel buildings that look like someone wanted to build a spaceship and got talked down to a research campus at the last minute.

Elias Thorne built it. He's been at this for decades. Patient, intentional, thinking in timelines that make everyone else look like they're planning lunch. I've known him for fifteen years, since he came up to me after a conference talk about quantum vacuum energy that the rest of the audience had politely slept through. Elias didn't sleep through it. He sat in the front row, leaned forward for the entire forty-five minutes, and afterward bought me a scotch and said, "You're right about all of it, and I can prove it."

He couldn't prove it. But he could fund it. And for a theoretical physicist whose theory nobody took seriously and who had a daughter to raise on an academic salary, funding was enough.

I trust Elias. I want to say that clearly. What I'm about to do is built on that trust.

The cat sits in the passenger seat, watching Texas go by.

"Big day, buddy."

He looks at me. One green eye.

Here's the theory. Twenty years of it, and I'm going to explain it to a cat in a car because he's the only audience I have who won't interrupt.

The ZPE field -- Lawson already knows the basics, the Casimir plates, the vacuum energy. What she doesn't know is the part I haven't published. The part that got me polite nods at conferences and impolite laughter behind my back.

The ZPE field isn't noise. It's structure. Organized along prime-number harmonics. And it connects things that shouldn't be connected -- parallel dimensions, each vibrating at its own frequency within the quantum vacuum. Like radio stations on the same electromagnetic spectrum. You can't hear FM with an AM receiver. You can't see another dimension from inside your own frequency.

Unless you build a tuner.

The State Bridge. Three stages: amplification, frequency tuning, superposition collapse. Amplify the field with superconducting coils until it's readable. Tune to a target frequency. Wrap the subject in a superposition state across both the origin and target dimension. Collapse toward the target.

The subject materializes in another reality.

Nobody's ever tested it on anything larger than a mathematical model. Today I'm testing it on me.

Schrodinger shifts in the passenger seat. He's been staring at me through this entire mental monologue, and there's something in that eye. Not understanding -- he's a cat, not a colleague. Patience, maybe. The look of a creature who's been listening to me talk about quantum mechanics for seven years and has learned to wait it out.

"If this works," I say, "everything changes. If it doesn't --"

I don't finish. There isn't really a good way to finish that sentence when your daughter is asleep at home and there's a coffee going cold on the counter next to a Post-it that might be the last normal thing you ever said to her.

He turns back to the window.


*


The State Bridge is ready.

Seven years of building. Longer if you count the theory, which I do, because theory is just practice without a budget.

The machine fills an entire room in the Institute's basement. Massive electromagnetic coils. A resonance chamber lined with superconducting material that Thorne sourced from contacts I've learned not to ask about. A control system I designed that Thorne's engineers built to specs so precise that three of them quit during the process. One cited "unreasonable expectations of physics" in his resignation letter. I had it framed.

I can feel the coils from here. Not with my hands -- with something deeper. A pressure in my chest. A subsonic hum that's more vibration than sound. The ZPE field is amplified to operational levels, and even though my body isn't designed to perceive it, something primal in me knows that the vacuum in this room is no longer behaving normally.

Elias is in the control room. He insisted on being here -- not just watching, but running the controls himself. I can see him through the observation window. Hands steady on the console. He has that look. The look of a man who wants the future so badly he can taste it. Same look he had over scotch.

I used to find it inspiring. Lately I find it... intense. The kind of focus that doesn't have room for doubt.

I don't finish the thought. Not today.

His voice comes over the intercom. Clipped, precise, faint British accent that shows up when he's excited. "Systems are nominal. Field is stable. Coils at full coherence. Cavity resonance is within operational parameters."

"Copy that."

I'm standing in the resonance chamber. The cat is in my arms.

This was not the plan. The plan was a tungsten test sphere. But Schrodinger walked into the chamber while I was doing final checks. Walked right in, hopped up on the main coil because it was warm, and settled himself down with the determined purposefulness of a cat who has found a good spot and will not be moved.

And I felt something. Like the start of a thought I hadn't finished.

I picked him up. I didn't put him down.

"Field strength at seventy percent and climbing." Elias again. "Frequency spectrum is resolving. I'm seeing the dimensional harmonics in the noise -- Henry, the theory is confirmed. The spectrum is structured exactly as you predicted. Prime-number intervals." A pause. I can hear him breathing through the intercom. "You should step out now. The sphere is loaded."

Part of me wants to stop and savor that. The spectrum is structured exactly as you predicted. Twenty years.

But I'm past savoring.

"Change of plan," I say.

Silence.

"Henry?"

"I'm going, Elias. Not the sphere. Me."

More silence.

"That's not what we discussed."

"No."

"The risks --"

"I know the risks." I do. The probability of successful transit is high. The probability of return is... less certain. The Bridge doesn't have a targeting system. If I go, I go wherever the tuner locks -- the strongest adjacent dimensional frequency. No way to choose. And no way to come back, because the Bridge only exists here, in this room, and wherever I end up won't have one.

But there's a note in a book on a shelf. And there's a cat in my arms whose purring is so steady it's almost like he knows something I don't.

"Henry, I have to recommend --"

"I know." I cut him off gently. "I know what you're going to say. And I appreciate it. But I'm going."

Schrodinger shifts in my arms. Purring against my chest. The resonance chamber is thrumming now -- I can feel the coils cycling at full power, the cavity reaching operational amplitude.

"Hit the button, Elias."

A pause. Three seconds. Four.

"Initiating."

The hum changes. Deepens. Not the operational hum of the coils -- something underneath that. Something that lives in the gaps between particles, between heartbeats. The zero-point field, amplified and tuned, locking onto the strongest adjacent dimensional frequency. Stage two complete. The resonance matrix has found a channel. I don't know which one.

Then stage three.

The light comes. Blue, then white, then something past white. A color I don't have a word for. I can feel my body trying to exist in two places at once. Not pain. Not exactly. More like the moment between sleeping and waking, stretched into something physical. My cells don't know which reality to commit to.

I think of Lawson.

I think of the note.

I think of Gayelyn.

I think: I hope she's angry. I hope she's angry enough to find that note and refuse to let it be the end. She will. She's smarter than me. She's always been smarter than me.

The superposition collapses. Not toward home.

The light swallows everything.

The last thing I hear is the cat purring.
