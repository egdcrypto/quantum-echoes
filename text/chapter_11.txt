CHAPTER 11 - THE NOTE


I'm burning the eggs.

Not a metaphor. Actual eggs, in a pan, on a stove, in a kitchen that belongs to a woman whose husband looked exactly like me and is now dead because Elias Thorne put his hand on his shoulder. I'm wearing the dead man's apron because Gayelyn said something about oil splatters on my only good shirt.

The eggs are on fire. Not figuratively. There are small flames.

I'm a physicist. I've published twenty-three peer-reviewed papers on quantum entanglement. I designed a portable Quantum State Bridge. I've survived two hundred and fourteen dimensions and catalogued alien species by bioluminescence index.

I cannot make scrambled eggs.

Schrodinger sits on the kitchen counter watching me fail. His one eye tracks the smoke with the alert intensity of a predator assessing whether the situation might produce food. The smoke alarm has been disabled since week two. Gayelyn did it without comment. She handles most things that way -- the gap between what I am and what her Henry was, managed quietly.

Her Henry could cook.

Of course he could.


*


Three months. I've been here three months.

Three months since the trail. Since Gayelyn said my name and the cat gave me away by purring. Since I stopped jumping, stopped running, stopped holding my breath before each new reality.

The cabin in the woods is empty now. I moved in six weeks ago, and it happened so gradually it felt less like a decision and more like gravity. First dinner at the house. Then dinners, plural. Then staying late. Then staying over on the couch because the cabin was far and Gayelyn said "just stay" like it was nothing, which we both knew it wasn't.

Couch became the guest room. Guest room became an awkward moment in the hallway outside the bedroom at 11 p.m., both of us standing there, not saying the thing.

She said it.

"The bed is big enough for two people, Henry. It always was."

She wasn't looking at me. Hand on the doorknob. Voice steady the way it gets when she's decided something and is done discussing it.

I sleep on the left side. She sleeps on the right. In my original reality it was opposite. I adjusted. You adjust to everything eventually. That's what humans do. We fill the available space. We learn to sleep on the wrong side of a bed that isn't ours and call it home.

The pod is in the lean-to behind the house. Tarped. Dusty. Haven't touched it in three months. The go-bag's in the closet. The Sharpie rules on the pod's interior wall are fading.

I'm not a multiverse traveler anymore. I'm a man who burns eggs.


*


Lawson is warming up to me.

"Warming up" is generous. More like: no longer treating me as an active threat. He's gone from crossed arms and monosyllables to something resembling cautious coexistence. A teenager's version of a peace treaty, expressed through shared meals and the occasional question that's actually a test.

"Dad used to help me with physics homework," he says one evening, dropping the textbook on the kitchen table with the specific thud of a teenager making a point. "You any good at physics?"

He knows I'm good at physics. The question is whether I'll step into the space his father left. Whether I'll pick up the book and sit down and explain things the way his Henry did, or whether I'll be the stranger who sleeps on the wrong side of the bed and sets breakfast on fire.

I pick up the textbook.

Quantum mechanics. Intro level. Page 47.

Superposition.

I almost laugh. I do not laugh.

I sit down.

"Okay," I say. "Imagine a coin spinning in the air."

He rolls his eyes. "Dad used the same analogy."

"Because it's a good analogy. What else did he use?"

"M&Ms."

"Do you have M&Ms?"

He stares at me. Then he gets up, goes to the pantry, comes back with a bag.

We sit at the kitchen table for two hours. Red M&Ms for one state, blue for another, green for the observer who collapses the wave function by looking.

Lawson is sharp. Way sharper than he lets on. He asks questions that go straight for the weak points -- things most undergrads take weeks to get to.

Actually, they're the same kinds of questions my Lawson used to ask. Different voice. Different body. But aimed at the same soft spots in the same theories.

That one rattles around in my head for a while.

At the end, he sweeps all the M&Ms into his hand and eats them.

"Same as Dad," he says.

Not a test this time. A small, grudging acknowledgment that whatever I am -- ghost, duplicate, interdimensional trespasser -- I am also, in some measurable way, his father.

I'll take it.

Schrodinger gets an M&M that rolled off the table. He bats it across the kitchen floor, chases it under the refrigerator, then sits in front of the refrigerator staring at it like it personally wronged him.

Lawson retrieves it.

He's already the cat's favorite person. I've been replaced. In two hundred and fourteen dimensions, no one has managed to usurp my relationship with that cat. A sixteen-year-old did it in six weeks with floor snacks.


*


One morning, the eggs come out particularly bad.

Not just burned. Somehow also raw in the center. I would have said that was thermodynamically impossible but the evidence is sitting in the pan looking at me. Burned on the outside, raw on the inside. Schrodinger's eggs. Both states simultaneously. Hilarious. Nobody's laughing.

I scrape the pan into the trash.

Gayelyn is in the doorway. She's been watching.

"Henry could make eggs," she says.

She doesn't mean me. She means him. Her Henry. The man who lived in this house and cooked breakfast and slept on the right side of the bed and died on the floor of his lab at forty-three.

For three months she's been patient. Warm. Generous in ways I don't deserve. And she hasn't said the thing that's been sitting behind her eyes.

Until now.

"I know," I say.

"He made them with chives. From the garden. Every Sunday."

She's not looking at me. She's looking at the pan.

"You don't even know where the garden is, do you."

Not really a question. I don't answer it.

"Three months." Her voice isn't loud. It's flat, which is worse. "Three months and I still have to tell you where things are in my own kitchen. I have to watch you figure out the coffee maker every morning like it's new technology. I have to listen to you breathe wrong at night."

"Breathe wrong?"

"He breathed slower. When he slept. You breathe like you're still running."

I set the pan down. She's right. I probably am still running, somewhere underneath, even asleep.

"And Lawson." She grips the edge of the counter. "Lawson is starting to call you Dad. Did you know that? Not to your face. To his friends. He told Marcus you were his dad and Marcus said I thought your dad died and Lawson said he came back."

She stops. Takes a breath.

"He came back, Henry. That's what my son is telling people. And you --" She stops again. "You are not back. You are someone else. Someone who looks like him and talks like him and uses the same M&M trick and sleeps in his bed and cannot make his eggs."

The kitchen is very quiet.

"What do you want me to say?" I ask.

"Something true. Something that isn't careful. Something that isn't you being gentle with me like I'm about to break."

"Okay." I look at her. "I can't make his eggs because I'm not him. I'm never going to be him. There are two hundred and fourteen dimensions between me and the man who knew where your garden is, and I can't close that gap. I'm not going to learn my way across it. And I know that."

I should stop. I don't.

"I stay because the alternative is a universe where you're dead and I'm alone and I already did that for seven years and I can't do it again."

"That's not fair to me," she says quietly.

"No."

"Or Lawson."

"No."

"And you did it anyway."

"Yes."

She stands there gripping the counter. She doesn't cry. She doesn't yell. She doesn't tell me to leave. She just looks at me with an expression I can't catalog, can't file in any of my careful inventories of difference. My Gayelyn died before she had the chance to be this angry at me.

"Make the eggs again," she says finally. "And use the chives. They're by the back door."

She leaves the kitchen.

I stand there for a long time.

Then I go find the chives.


*


Late at night. Gayelyn asleep. Lawson's light off. House quiet.

I think about the note.

Lawson, it's not you, it's not me, it's Schrodinger.

Nine words. Written at a kitchen table in a reality I can't get back to, on a morning when the universe tapped me on the shoulder and I wrote down what I thought it was saying.

Here's the thing about that morning: I didn't plan it. I didn't sit down and think, "I should leave a coded message for my daughter in case I get flung into another dimension by a machine I built with a man who might be the most dangerous person in the multiverse."

That would have been smart. Strategic.

I just wrote it.

The way I always wrote notes for her. Don't forget you're extraordinary. Your old man loves you. The universe is weird and so are we. Little scraps stuck into the pages of our shared book. Breadcrumbs from a man who was bad at saying things out loud and getting worse at it every year.

And that morning -- the morning of the experiment -- I wrote nine words that could be a joke, or a clue, or a lifeline, depending on who found them.

Lawson is smart. She'll find the note. She's probably already found it. Probably been staring at it for years, turning those nine words like a Rubik's cube.

She'll figure out it's about superposition. The cat. Alive and dead until someone opens the box.

Will she rebuild the Bridge? Crack the DNA resonance? Follow the trail?

Part of me hopes yes. Part of me hopes no. Because if she does -- if my daughter takes the note and the book and the empty lab and turns them into a doorway -- she'll come looking for me.

And she'll find me here.

In this kitchen. Burning eggs for a woman who isn't her mother. In a house with a boy who isn't her but has her DNA. Living a life I built on top of the one I owed her.

She won't understand. Why would she.

I don't understand it myself. I don't have a defense. It's the most selfish thing I've ever done and I did it because I found my wife again and I was too weak to walk away twice.

That's not a reason. That's a confession.


*


She's thirty-five now. Best estimate, if time in the original dimension has passed the way I think it has. No way to verify. Thirty-five. Same age Gayelyn was when the cancer got her.

What is she doing? Still at the university? Did she go into physics? Is she happy? Is she alone?

I don't know. I lie in the dark and I don't know.


*


Gayelyn tucks her hair behind her ear in her sleep. Same gesture. The one I fell in love with twenty-five years ago in a university library when a girl across the table did it and I thought: that one. She does it unconsciously. A reflex so deep it works across dimensions.

She is the same and she is not.

There are differences. Coffee black here, cream and sugar in my reality. Jazz here, blues in mine. Scar on her left knee from a childhood fall that happened differently -- longer, more jagged. I've catalogued these the way I catalogued dimensions. Not to find fault. To map the distance. The gap is narrow, but it's there, and at night on the wrong side of the bed, I feel it.

My Gayelyn died at thirty-one. This Gayelyn is alive at forty-five.

Fourteen extra years. Lines around the eyes. Graying hair. I'm grateful for every wrinkle because each one is a day she didn't die.

You can't say that to someone without explaining the multiverse and the cancer and seven years of reaching for the wrong side of an empty bed. So I don't.


*


The cat sleeps at the foot of the bed.

This is new. In the cabin, he slept on my chest. In the pod, against my side. But here he's picked the foot of the bed. Equidistant from both of us. Neutral territory. Switzerland.

He's not my cat anymore. He's the household cat. Lawson feeds him. Gayelyn lets him on the counter, which I never allowed and which she insists is fine. He's claimed a spot on the couch. The golden retriever learned this the hard way. One eye, four pounds lighter than the dog, zero fear. He runs this house.

He's happy here.

That should be enough. Mostly it is.

But I wonder about the other one. The other him. The Schrodinger in the original dimension. The one my Lawson has. Because the cat is still in both places. One cat, two realities, a superposition that shows no sign of collapsing. I feel it sometimes -- a faint doubling in his energy, a shimmer the HUD used to pick up and that I can still sense if I focus.

He's with Lawson right now. On a stack of journals, probably. Blinking his one eye. Judging her.

I hope he's taking care of her.


*


The eggs are burned.

I scrape them onto three plates. They have chives this time, which doesn't make them good but makes them different. Gayelyn takes hers with the expression of someone choosing not to comment. Lawson takes his with the resignation of a man who has accepted his fate. Schrodinger gets a piece of toast crust, which he sniffs, considers, and accepts.

We eat at the kitchen table. Man, woman, boy, cat. A family built from spare parts.

Gayelyn reaches for the salt. Tucks her hair behind her ear.

Lawson asks me something about entanglement. I explain it with the salt and pepper shakers because we're out of M&Ms.

Schrodinger sits on the empty fourth chair.

There are only three of us but the chair is there and the cat is on it and I'm thinking about my Lawson at a different table in a different dimension. Whether she's eating breakfast alone.

I hope not. I hope someone brings her tacos or whatever she eats for breakfast these days. I hope there's someone across from her who sees what she is.

I eat my burned eggs. I don't taste them.

I think: Lawson, it's not you, it's not me, it's Schrodinger.

I know what those words mean now. Both alive and dead. Both here and there. I'm sitting at a table with a woman I love and a boy who's learning to trust me and a cat who has usurped me in every way.

And in another reality I'm the empty chair.

Both states. Simultaneously.

That's the answer, kiddo. I'm both. The father who stayed and the father who left. The man in the kitchen and the ghost in the lab. And the cat is in both places, and the box never opens, because you and I are in different realities looking at different versions of the same thing.

Unless you get here. Unless you open the box.

I hope you're coming. I don't deserve it.

But I hope.


-- End of Chapter 11 --
